# Do Your Market Research (Compile the Truth)

When this chapter is done you will have one document called your Voice of the Customer sheet. It holds the exact words of 5 to 10 real people who might buy from you. You sort those words into groups we call buckets: their fears, their daily frustrations, their dream outcome, the exact phrases they use, their objections, and the price they named. It is not your opinion about your customer. It is your customer, in their own words, on one page. Every chapter after this one reads from this sheet. If you only do one thing right in this whole playbook, do this one.

Here is the rule the entire playbook stands on: nothing in a business should be invented. It should be compiled from real people. You do not guess what your customer feels. You go find out, you write down their actual sentences, and then you build everything on top of those sentences. The next chapter (a guided interview that turns your customer's words into a brand story, called StoryBrand) looks like it starts from thin air. It does not. It secretly assumes you already know your customer's exact words. This chapter is where you go and get those words.

This playbook runs on a method called CASE: Compile, Analyze, Strategize, Execute. This chapter is the Compile step. You gather the raw truth here. Later chapters Analyze it (turn raw words into a brand), Strategize on it (build the offer and the website), and Execute (make content and get customers). We are not going to claim who first named CASE. If a source ever gets credited for it in a later edition, that credit gets verified before publishing. For now, treat CASE as the operating order of the work: you cannot analyze what you have not compiled.

## What you need

| Thing | What it is | Cost | Time |
|---|---|---|---|
| A way to reach 5 to 10 potential customers | People who might actually buy what you plan to sell. Warm ones first (people you or your family already know). | Free | A few days to line up |
| A video call app with recording | Zoom, Google Meet, or similar. Recording lets you capture exact words instead of scribbling. | Free plans work | Ongoing |
| A transcription tool | An app that turns the recording into text you can read and search (Otter.ai is one; any works). | Free plans work | Ongoing |
| A notebook or one document | Where your Voice of the Customer sheet lives. Google Docs, Notes, or a plain text file. Kept forever. | Free | Ongoing |
| A parent or trusted adult | In the loop for every call, because you are a teenager contacting adults and recording them. | Free | 1 to 2 hours |
| About 15 minutes per call | Each research call is short on purpose. People say yes to 15 minutes. | Free | 15 min each, plus a few minutes to file |

Almost every cost here is $0. The real cost is nerve: picking up the phone and asking real people real questions. That nerve is the whole skill.

## Step 1: Learn the one rule before you dial anyone

**What to do.** Burn this into your head: you are a reporter, not a salesperson. Your only job on these calls is to collect the truth and write it down word for word. You are not there to pitch, to defend an idea, or to get anyone to like your plan. You are there to find out what is actually wrong in their life and how they talk about it.

This is why the method is called Compile. A reporter does not invent quotes. A reporter records what was said. Everything you build later (your brand, your offer, your website, your posts) is only as honest as the raw material you gather now. Garbage in, garbage forever.

**How Jay did it.** Before Jay wrote a single word of the Outsmart the System brand, he ran market research calls with parents of teenagers. Those calls came first. The brand came second, built from what the parents said. In the next chapter you will meet a character named Daniel, the one imaginary parent Jay uses to make every decision. Daniel is not made up. Jay's own files describe the hero as a blend of real interviewed parents ("a synthesis of real interviewed parents"). Daniel exists because these calls happened first. No calls, no Daniel, no brand.

**Now you.** Write this sentence at the top of your document: "I am compiling my customer's exact words. I am not inventing anything." You will reread it every time you are tempted to guess.

## Step 2: Pick who to talk to, and list 5 to 10 of them

**What to do.** Decide who your offer is for, then list 5 to 10 real people who fit. Start with warm contacts: people you or your family already know, or people a friend can introduce you to. Warm people say yes faster and talk more openly. You want at least 5 real conversations. Ten is better. Fewer than 5 and you are still guessing.

Your customer is whoever your offer is for. For Jay it was parents of teenagers. For you it might be dog owners on your street, small shop owners downtown, other teens who game, or busy parents who need a lawn service. Point the same questions at whoever your customer actually is.

**How Jay did it.** Jay's customer was clear from the start: parents raising teenagers. He did not survey strangers on the internet. He had real conversations with parents who fit, then blended several of them into one composite (one made-up person built from many real ones). His story rule, quoted from his own library, means hide who each person is: "Pseudonymize ruthlessly. Change names, dollar amounts, ages, locations. Only the emotional truth stays."

**Now you.** Write down 5 to 10 names. Next to each, note how you know them and one reason they fit your customer. Then hand the list to a parent or trusted adult. Any call with an adult you do not already know well, your parent helps set up and sits in on. That is a standing rule in this playbook: for anything where a teen contacts adults, records a call, or handles money, a trusted adult is in the loop.

## Step 3: Frame the call honestly (the opener)

**What to do.** Open every call by telling the truth about what it is: a research call, not a sales call, about 15 minutes. Say it plainly and mean it. When people know you are not about to sell them anything, they relax and tell you the real stuff. That honesty is not a trick to lower their guard. It is the actual deal. You really are only there to learn.

Here is Jay's real opener. Copy it and swap in your own customer and topic. Notice there is no em dash anywhere in it, because there are none anywhere in this playbook.

**COPY AND ADAPT: The honest opener**

```
Hi [Name], thank you so much for joining me today. I'm really
excited to learn more about your experience with [their situation,
e.g. raising a teenager today]. This will help me understand how to
better support [who you serve]. As promised, this won't take more
than 15 minutes, and this is strictly a research call, not a sales
call. Does that sound good?

Great. To make the best use of our time, I'll ask you some
questions about [the topic]. Please share as honestly and openly
as you can. When you've finished a thought, just let me know and
I'll move to the next question.
```

A softer version works too, especially for a first-timer: "This isn't a sales call. I'm gathering honest feedback so I can build something that actually helps. I'd love your real input so I can get it right."

**How Jay did it.** Jay's script opens by promising 15 minutes and stating flat out that it is "strictly a research call, not a sales call." That single line does more work than anything else in the call. It sets the honest terms and it keeps him honest too.

**Now you.** Write your own opener from the template. Read it out loud until it sounds like you and not like a form letter. If any part of it is not true (if you are secretly planning to pitch), delete that part. The opener has to be real.

## Step 4: Ask permission, then record

**What to do.** Before you record anything, ask. A recording lets you capture exact words instead of half-remembering them later, which is the entire point of this chapter. But you never record a person without their clear yes.

**COPY AND ADAPT: The recording-consent ask**

```
Do you mind if I record this so I can listen back later instead of
scribbling notes while you talk? It's just for me, so I remember
your exact words. I won't share it publicly.
```

If they say no, that is fine. Take written notes instead and get their sentences down as close to word for word as you can. Recording-consent rules are different from place to place, and some places require everyone to agree before a call is recorded. So you always ask, every time. Asking is both the legal-safe move and the honest one.

**How Jay did it.** Jay's execution tip comes straight from his script. Run the calls on a video app and use a transcription tool to record them. Then you can "circle the exact phrases and complaints the parents use so you can mirror that language" later. The workflow is simple: record the call, run it through transcription to get text, then go through the text and highlight the exact phrases the person used. Those highlighted phrases are gold. They become the literal words on your website.

**Now you.** Set up a free video call app and a free transcription tool before your first call. Do a 60-second test recording of yourself and confirm the transcript comes out readable. Then, on every real call, ask permission first using the template above. Get the yes before you hit record.

## Step 5: Find the bleed (ask, then go silent)

**What to do.** Now the heart of the call. Ask about their pain, one question at a time, then stop talking. Completely. Let them fill the silence. This is called active listening: you ask, then you get out of the way and let the other person talk, even if it feels awkward, even if they ramble for four minutes. The awkward silence is where the real answers live.

You are listening for what Jay's script calls "the bleed." The bleed is the ongoing problem that quietly costs your customer money, sleep, or peace, day after day, the thing that never quite gets fixed. People will pay to stop a bleed. Your job right now is only to find it and write down how they describe it.

Here are the core questions, taken straight from Jay's real parent script. Ask them in your own customer's language. For a different business, keep the shape and swap the subject (the dental version and the lawn version ask the very same questions about a very different bleed).

**COPY AND ADAPT: The bleed questions**

```
- What are you struggling with the most right now when it comes to
  [their situation]?
- When you think about [their future concern], what keeps you up at
  night?
- What are you most afraid of when it comes to [the thing they
  worry about]?
- What are your top three daily frustrations with [the situation]
  right now?
- Over the past year, what has been your most present, persistent
  problem with [the situation]?
- How do you currently try to solve this problem?
- What happens if you don't solve it?
```

When an answer is vague, do not rescue them and do not feed them an idea. If you suggest the answer, you have contaminated your own research. Instead use these three probes, exactly as written in the script. Each one hands the talking back to them without putting words in their mouth.

**COPY AND ADAPT: The three probes that never feed an idea**

```
- How so?
- Can you tell me more about that?
- How did that make you feel?
```

**How Jay did it.** Jay's script is blunt about the silence: after you ask a question, "you must not interrupt or offer suggestions. Let the parent fill the silence with their frustrations. If they vent for 4 minutes, let them vent." The venting is the data. The person who talks the most teaches you the most.

**Now you.** Practice the silence before your first real call. Ask a family member one of these questions and then say nothing for a full ten seconds after they stop. It will feel like forever. Get used to it. On the real calls, your best material shows up in the second and third sentence, after the silence pulls it out.

## Step 6: Ask for the dream (the magic wand)

**What to do.** Once you have found the bleed, ask them to describe the cure. This is the single most useful question in the whole call, because it makes the customer describe your product for you, in their words, before you have built anything.

**COPY AND ADAPT: The magic wand question**

```
If I could wave a magic wand and instantly fix your biggest
struggle with [the situation], what would that perfect solution
look like for you?

(then probe) How would that change your day to day?
```

Their answer is your feature list. Whatever they describe is what your offer should try to deliver. You did not invent the offer. They handed it to you.

Then, if you have time, add these four extra questions. Each one unlocks a different piece of your future business. These come from a fuller version of the script.

**COPY AND ADAPT: The four extra questions (value, price, objections)**

```
- Have you ever tried programs, courses, or other solutions for
  this? What stopped you, or what made you stop? (reveals what your
  competition is and why it fails them)
- What would make something like this a no-brainer for you?
  (reveals the exact promise that wins them over)
- If you had to guess, what would you expect to pay for something
  that solved this? (gives you a real price anchor, a number a real
  customer said out loud)
- What might hold you back from trying something like this?
  (reveals the objections you'll need to answer later)
```

**How Jay did it.** Jay's script calls the magic wand question the one that lets "the prospect literally tell you exactly what features your program needs to offer." He does not brainstorm features alone at a desk. He collects them from the people who will pay.

**Now you.** Ask the magic wand question and write the answer down word for word. If the call is going well, ask the four extras too. The price answer in particular is worth its weight in gold. You will use that number in the offer chapter, and it will be a real number a real person named, not a price you guessed.

## Step 7: Close honestly, and only book a next step if it is real

**What to do.** End by thanking them, telling them plainly what you heard, and stopping there. If, and only if, you genuinely intend to build something for them, you may ask whether they would like you to come back and show them what you make. That is an honest offer. It is not a trap.

**COPY AND ADAPT: The honest close**

```
Thank you so much. This was exactly what I needed. What I'm hearing
is that [reflect back their real words, e.g. the constant battle
over screen time and the fear it won't get better]. I'm not selling
anything today, I'm just learning. If it's okay with you, may I keep
you posted as I build this? No pressure at all.
```

Here is the honesty warning you have to hear. The scripts this chapter is built from call the close "setting the trap" for a follow-up "sales presentation." They tell you to lock in a day and time before you hang up. Do not do that version. A research call that is secretly a sales setup is a lie, the exact kind of dishonesty the Rules of the Game chapter bans. If you promised "this is not a sales call" in your opener and then spring a pitch, you broke your word inside 15 minutes.

The honest version is simple. You booked a real research call, you did real research, and you leave it there. Booking a next conversation is fine, but only in one case: you truly plan to build something for this person, and you are inviting them to see it. It is not fine if you are cornering them into a sales meeting they never agreed to. As a teenager just starting out, you probably do not even have a product yet, so your honest close is almost always "May I keep you posted?" and nothing more. Offer, never trap.

**How Jay did it.** Jay's script does contain the "set the trap" language. This playbook keeps Jay's questions and throws out that move on purpose. The questions are excellent. The trap is not honest. We use one and refuse the other.

**Now you.** Write your close from the honest template. Practice saying "I'm not selling anything today" and meaning it. If you ever feel the urge to pivot into a pitch, stop, and remember that the trust you keep is worth more than any single sale.

## Step 8: Compile the Voice of the Customer sheet

**What to do.** After the calls, pull all your transcripts and notes into one sheet. Read through every call and drop each exact phrase into a bucket. Do not paraphrase. Copy their actual words. This sorted sheet is the deliverable of the whole chapter, the thing every later chapter reads from.

The buckets are: their fears, their daily frustrations, their dream outcome, the exact words they use, their objections, and the price they named. The full fill-in template is at the bottom of this chapter under "Your Voice of the Customer sheet."

**How Jay did it.** Jay's whole method is to record, transcribe, then "circle the exact phrases and complaints" and "mirror that language" in the finished offer. The mirroring only works if you kept the exact words. The moment you summarize "she was frustrated about screens," you have thrown away the sentence that would have sold the product. Keep the sentence.

**Now you.** Open your document and build the six buckets. Go call by call and paste real phrases under each one, with a tag for which person said it (use a fake initial to protect their privacy, like "Parent A"). By the end you should have a page or two of real human sentences, sorted. That page is worth more than any idea you could have dreamed up alone.

## Step 9: The bridge (how these words become your brand)

**What to do.** Understand what you just built, because the next chapter runs on it directly. The StoryBrand interview asks you for three kinds of problem. You already have the answers, in your customers' own words, sitting in your buckets. Here is the exact hand-off.

- What they said is going wrong (from the struggle and frustration questions) becomes your **external problem**, the visible thing you could point a camera at.
- How they said it makes them feel (from "what keeps you up at night," "what are you most afraid of," and "how did that make you feel?") becomes your **internal problem**, the private feeling underneath.
- The bigger "it is just wrong that things are this way" underneath their answers becomes your **philosophical problem**, the belief your brand stands for.
- A single real quote from your sheet becomes the exact sentence on your website. Not a sentence you wrote. A sentence they said.
- The price someone named becomes the starting anchor for your offer.
- The objections you collected become the questions your sales page has to answer before anyone will buy.

This is how the "never invent anything" rule actually works in practice. You are not being asked to be honest by willpower alone. You are being handed a system where the honest move is also the easy move, because the words are already there. The composite parent you meet in the next chapter, the one Jay makes every decision through, was compiled from calls exactly like the ones you just ran. He was not invented at a desk. He was assembled from real sentences. Yours will be too.

**How Jay did it.** Jay's finished brand is full of oddly specific, real-sounding lines because they came from real parents. When a parent reads one of those lines on his page, they stop scrolling, because it is the sentence they have thought a hundred times and never seen written down. That only happens when the sentence was compiled, not composed.

**Now you.** Before you start the next chapter, skim your Voice of the Customer sheet and mark the three or four sentences that hit hardest, the ones that gave you a small chill when you heard them. Those are your best raw material. You will build your whole brand around them.

## Proof this works for any business

You might think this only works because Jay was selling to parents, an emotional topic. It is not about the topic. The exact same four-phase call (frame it honestly, find the bleed, ask for the dream, close honestly) works for any business, pointed at any customer.

Another operator took this identical script and aimed it at a completely different market: owners of dental practices, for a marketing agency. Same four phases. Same silence. Same magic wand question. The only thing that changed was the subject of the questions. Instead of asking a parent what keeps them up at night about their teenager, it asked a practice owner what keeps them up at night. For them the worry was money: they spend on ads but not enough new patients actually book. Instead of asking a parent to describe their dream for their kid, it asked a practice owner to describe their dream for their front desk. The structure did not change. The bleed was different, so the words were different, and that is exactly the point: you are not inventing the words, you are collecting whatever real words that particular customer gives you.

So when you sit down to point these four phases at your own business, trust the structure. Change the subject of every question to fit your customer. Keep the frame, keep the silence, keep the magic wand, keep the honest close. The method does not care what you sell. It only cares that you go and compile the truth.

## Words you just learned

- **CASE**: the method this playbook runs on: Compile, Analyze, Strategize, Execute. You compile the truth first, then build on it in that order.
- **Compile**: gathering real words from real people instead of inventing them. The job of this whole chapter.
- **Market research**: talking to real potential customers to learn how they think, feel, and speak, before you build anything.
- **Voice of the Customer sheet**: the one-page document holding your customers' exact words, sorted into buckets. The deliverable of this chapter and the fuel for every chapter after it.
- **The bleed**: the ongoing problem that quietly costs your customer money, sleep, or peace, day after day. People pay to stop a bleed.
- **Active listening**: asking a question, then staying silent so the other person fills the space with the real answer.
- **Probe**: a short follow-up ("How so?") that hands the talking back to the customer without putting words in their mouth.
- **Magic wand question**: asking a customer to describe their perfect solution, so they hand you your feature list in their own words.
- **Warm contact**: a person you or your family already know, or can be introduced to. Warm people say yes faster and talk more openly.
- **Recording consent**: clear permission, asked for before you hit record, every time. Rules vary by place, so you always ask.
- **Transcription**: turning a recorded call into readable text you can search and highlight.
- **Price anchor**: a real number a real customer named when asked what they would pay. It becomes the starting point for your offer.
- **Objection**: the reason a customer might not buy. Collect them now so your sales page can answer them later.
- **Composite**: one realistic made-up person built by blending several real people you actually talked to. Real emotional truth, no real identity.
- **StoryBrand**: the guided interview in the next chapter that turns your customer's exact words into a simple brand story. Your Voice of the Customer sheet is what feeds it.
- **External / internal / philosophical problem**: what is visibly going wrong / how it makes the customer feel privately / why the whole situation is just wrong. You gather the raw words here; the next chapter sorts them into these three.
- **The trap (what not to do)**: pretending to run a research call while secretly setting up a sales pitch. Banned. A research call is a real research call.
