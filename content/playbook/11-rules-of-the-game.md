# Rules of the Game: Honesty, Safety, and Credit

**What you will have when this chapter is done:** a one-page rules file for your business, a claims checklist that can block a post from publishing, a written consent process for telling real people's stories, and the habit that replaces every unproven number with the words "verify before publishing."

---

| Your offer file | The one document where your prices and claims live (you built this when you designed your offer) | Free | Already exists |

No software to install. No accounts to create. This chapter is about words, not tools.

---

## Why this chapter exists

Everything else in this playbook builds an asset. This chapter protects it.

The asset is not your logo, your website, or your product. The asset is trust. People buy from a teenager they trust. Nobody buys from a teenager who got caught making something up. And here is the brutal math: trust takes months to build and one fake claim to destroy. One invented statistic, one fake "only 3 spots left," one made-up customer quote. The internet screenshots everything. You do not get the trust back.

Jay Bhakta learned to treat honesty as a system, not a mood. He did not just decide to be honest. He wrote the rules down, made them non-negotiable, and built a gate that blocks a post from going out if it breaks them. This chapter walks you through building the same system for your business.

## Step 1. Write your standing rules file before you publish anything

**What to do:** Create one document that lists your non-negotiable rules. Anyone who helps you make content (a friend, a parent, an AI assistant) reads this file before writing a word for your business.

**How Jay did it:** Jay keeps a standing rules file that every content session must obey. The rules are written as bans, not suggestions. The core ones, quoted from his file:

> "No fabricated stats, quotes, names, dates, or sources. If a stat isn't verified, don't invent it. Say 'verify before publishing' instead."

> "No fake scarcity, no fake urgency, no fake testimonials."

Notice the design. The rule does not say "try to be accurate." It names the exact things people fabricate (stats, quotes, names, dates, sources) and bans each one. And it gives you the replacement move: when you do not have proof, you write the flag "verify before publishing" right where the number would go. Honesty becomes a workflow step instead of a hope.

Why written down? Because rules you only remember bend when you are excited. Rules on paper do not. Jay's file also means any AI tool he uses inherits the rules automatically. The AI reads the file first, so it cannot "helpfully" invent a statistic for him.

**Now you:** Open a blank document. Title it "My Rules." Write these five lines and add your own:

*Copy and adapt:*

```
MY RULES (non-negotiable)
1. No fabricated stats, quotes, names, dates, or sources.
   If I can't verify it, I write "verify before publishing" instead.
2. No fake scarcity, no fake urgency, no fake testimonials.
3. Education only. I never tell anyone what to buy or promise results.
4. Real people's stories need written permission before I publish them.
5. I credit people by name when I use their ideas.
```

Show it to your parent or mentor. Then treat it like a law, not a suggestion.

## Step 2. Make "verify before publishing" a reflex

**What to do:** Every time a number, quote, or fact wants to go into your content, ask: can I prove this right now? If not, it does not publish as a fact. It publishes as a flag, or not at all.

**How Jay did it:** Three real examples from Jay's own work.

First, when Jay reused his old Facebook posts, he reread every one and deleted anything he could not prove. That reread cut lines like "32% of teens don't know credit vs debit" and "smart kids make $50K+ in financial mistakes." Not because they were definitely false. Because nobody could produce the source. The rule was: rewrite the post without the number, or carry the tag "[STAT, verify source before publishing]" until someone finds the receipt.

Second, one of his own beliefs carries the note "never assert a number from memory." Your memory is not a source.

Third, and this is the important one. Jay once had a single line of notes from a call with a mentor. That was too thin to build a post from. Instead of writing the post anyway and making up details the mentor never said, he parked the idea with a note: do not invent his examples. Parked (Jay's word for it is HELD) means the idea waits until he has real material. A parked idea costs you nothing. An invented detail can cost you everything.

The hardest part: Jay applied this to his own story. His own list of banned claims (more on this file in Step 6) blocked lines like "20+ interviews" and "50+ families coached" until each count was individually documented with receipts. His own resume had to pass his own gate. The founder bio that survived is the one with no client counts or results to defend. The only numbers left are his own age and years in business, facts he can prove. Quoted here with the long dash in his original removed:

> "I spent 20 years building businesses and was financially illiterate for most of my career. I learned this the hard way at 38. I built the program I wish someone had run for my own kids."

**Now you:** Go through anything you have already written for your business. Circle every number, every quote, every "studies show." For each one, either attach the source, delete it, or replace it with the flag. Then adopt the rule going forward: no number from memory, ever.

## Step 3. Ban fake scarcity and fake urgency

**What to do:** Never claim something is limited unless it is physically, actually limited. No countdown that resets. No "only 5 left" that never changes.

**How Jay did it:** Jay used to have counters like "5 founding families" (a special early-customer deal) and "first 20 families" in his marketing text. (Marketers call that text "copy.") He retired all of them. Why? A scarcity counter that never moves is a lie the internet can screenshot. If your page says "3 spots left" for six weeks, one screenshot comparison proves you lied, and every other claim you ever make gets doubted too.

What replaced it: scarcity is allowed only when it is calendar-true or capacity-true. His approved phrasings:

> "1:1 kickoffs limited by real calendar capacity."
> "Seats limited by the room."

Both of those are just facts. A calendar has a fixed number of hours. A room has a fixed number of chairs.

And when Jay wanted to say "Only 10 founding-family spots," he did not just start saying it. He made a dated ruling (a written decision with the date on it, 2026-07-19) that the cap is a genuine, enforced limit, and committed to honoring it in practice: when 10 sell, enrollment closes. The number is only legal to say because buyer number 11 genuinely cannot buy.

**Now you:** Look at your offer. Do you personally deliver something (coaching calls, custom orders, dog-walking hours)? Then you have real capacity, and you may say so plainly: "I can take on 4 clients this month because I'm also in school." That is honest, and honest limits are the only ones you can state without risk. What you may never do: invent a limit, or state a real limit you do not intend to enforce. The test is simple. If someone asked to be customer number 5 when you said 4, would you actually say no? If not, do not say 4.

## Step 4. Stay on the education side of the line

**What to do:** If your business touches money, health, or anything with real stakes, teach how things work. Never tell a specific person what to do with their money, and never promise results.

**How Jay did it:** Jay's business teaches teens and families about money, which puts him one step away from regulated territory. Financial advice, tax advice, and investment recommendations are licensed professions. Cross that line without a license and you are breaking the law, not just a brand rule. So he built hard guardrails:

- "Education only. Never give financial, tax, legal, or investment advice."
- Never recommend specific stocks or funds. When teaching how investing works, he uses "companies you already use" framing (talk about the idea of businesses people know, never "buy this one").
- No performance promises. Nobody can promise what a market will do.
- Paper trading first. Paper trading means practicing with pretend money before any real dollar moves.
- Parent always in the loop. A business that serves minors routes everything through the parent. The teen is never sold to alone.

He also prints the line on the page itself, so no visitor can mistake what they are buying. Verbatim compliance lines from his offer pages:

> "Entrepreneurship coaching, not financial advice."
> "Illustrative only. Not a promise of future performance." (on a calculator)
> "This is a money-and-momentum snapshot, not therapy, mental-health treatment, or a clinical assessment." (on his AI-guided teen tool, which also discloses that it is AI)

One more layer: Jay banned clinical language he is not licensed to use. "Prevent trauma" was removed everywhere. "Nervous-system level" was replaced with plain behavioral language. He even softened his own founding tagline for public use. If a phrase sounds like a doctor or therapist wrote it, and you are not one, it goes.

**Now you:** Write your own version of the compliance line and put it on anything you sell. Fill in the blank: "This is ________, not ________." Examples: "Study help, not a guarantee of grades." "Fitness ideas for teens, not medical advice." Then check your promises. Cross out anything that guarantees an outcome you do not control. If your business touches money, adopt Jay's rules wholesale: education only, no picks, no promises, practice before real dollars, parents in the loop.

## Step 5. Get written permission before you tell anyone's story

**What to do:** Real stories about real people are your most powerful content. They are also the easiest way to betray someone. The rule: written consent BEFORE any public use, and until you have it, the story runs anonymized (told with every identifying detail removed, like "a 15-year-old in our community").

**How Jay did it:** Jay built two consent tracks, one for adults and one for minors.

**Track 1: adults.** When Jay wanted to quote parents he had interviewed, he sent each one a personal email. Not a mass campaign. One personal email per person, from his own address. The email's key moves, from his actual template:

*Copy and adapt (the consent-ask email):*

- Subject line (adapted from Jay's, which uses a long dash): A small ask, and a thank you
- Open with one specific, real detail from your conversation with them ("you said something that stuck with me: [detail]"). This proves you were listening.
- Name why you are asking. Jay's line: the thing people trust isn't my opinion, it's what actually happened in real families.
- Ask for ONE anonymized line, and show them a sample of exactly what it would look like published.
- Give two ways to say yes: "Reply 'yes, use what fits' and I'll send you the exact wording for approval before anything goes out," or "write the line yourself and I'll use your words."
- Give a real way to say no: reply "pass" and that's completely fine. The conversation stays between us either way.

Notice the design. The person approves the exact wording. The attribution never uses their name (Jay's format: "mother of a 16-year-old, Phoenix"). And the "pass" option is genuine, which is exactly why people say yes.

**Track 2: minors.** For a story about a person under 18, an email is not enough. Jay created a signed release form (his first was drafted for a teen in his community, 2026-07-09). The rule at the top: one signed copy per person before ANY public use of their name, likeness, or identifiable story. Until it is signed, the story runs anonymized, as in "a 15-year-old in our community." The form's structure:

*Copy and adapt (the release form structure, then have a lawyer review it):*

1. Who is agreeing: your business, the participant, and (for a minor) the parent or guardian.
2. The exact approved story text, in a box. What is signed is what may be published. Any real change to the wording (anything beyond fixing a typo) requires a new sign-off.
3. Checkboxes for where it may appear: website, social media, email, ads, presentations.
4. Four terms, one per line:
   - No payment is owed for the story.
   - They can withdraw future use anytime, in writing. Already-published copies may not be retractable.
   - No misrepresentation. You never add claims, numbers, or outcomes beyond the approved box.
   - Accuracy. The signers confirm the story is true.
5. Three signatures: participant, parent or guardian, you.

Two warnings baked into Jay's version. First, withdrawal only covers future use, because the internet does not forget. That is why the gate sits BEFORE first publication, not after. Second, laws about using a minor's name, face, or story in public (lawyers call these publicity rights) vary by state, so the template itself says: have an attorney review this wording before you rely on it.

One more rule from Jay's system: a signed story is frozen. You cannot punch it up later. And testimonials are quote-exact. Jay has signed releases for exactly six customer quotes. Those six may be used, word for word. Any other quote, or any change to those six, needs a new signed release first. Count your own signed releases the same way: that number is your entire usable testimonial supply.

**Now you:** Before you publish any story involving a real person (a customer, a friend, your cousin), send the permission email. If the person is under 18, use the release form and get the parent's signature too. Until the yes arrives in writing, publish the anonymized version only: change names, ages, dollar amounts, and locations. Keep only the emotional truth. Jay's phrase for this is "pseudonymize ruthlessly."

## Step 6. Turn your rules into a publish gate

**What to do:** A rule that lives in a document but never stops anything is a wish. Turn your rules into a gate: a checklist you run before every post, and if the post fails, it does not go out. Period.

**How Jay did it:** Jay's rules file has a section titled, in effect, "Claims Policy (HARD GATE: a violation blocks publishing)." It opens: "Content is legally and reputationally exposed. These rules are non-negotiable and apply to every post, email, page, bio, and the PDF." In plain words: one bad post can get you sued or get you distrusted, so these rules can stop a post cold. The banned list includes any specific count of clients or results until each is individually documented, any partnership or credential he cannot prove in writing, and clinical language.

The gate connects to one more idea: a single source of truth. Every price, claim, refund promise, and link for Jay's business lives in ONE file. The rule at the top of that file, adapted here:

> Every piece of content (posts, emails, bios, web pages) quotes THIS file. Never type a price, scarcity claim, credential number, or link directly into a post from memory. Always copy it from THIS file. If this file and any other doc conflict, this file wins. When the offer changes: update this file FIRST, then regenerate copy.

Why? Because when your price lives in twenty places, one of them is always stale, and a stale price on a public page is a broken promise. With one file, you can never contradict yourself.

And the enforcement line, verbatim:

> "Any post/email/page that quotes a retired price/URL, a banned claim, or a never-moved scarcity counter is a bug, not content."

Read that again. A violation is not "content that needs a tweak." It is a bug. Bugs get fixed before anything ships.

Last piece: rules only change by dated ruling. When Jay changes a rule, he writes the date and the decision into the source-of-truth file, then updates the copy. Nothing changes silently. That means you can always answer "why does our copy say this?" with a date and a reason.

**Now you:** Build your gate. It is a 6-question checklist you run on every post before it publishes:

*Copy and adapt (the publish gate):*

```
BEFORE THIS POST GOES OUT:
1. Every number in it: do I have the source? (No source = cut it or flag it)
2. Every quote: is it word-for-word real, with permission?
3. Any scarcity or urgency claim: is it physically true and enforced?
4. Any real person's story: is written consent on file, or is it anonymized?
5. Price and offer details: do they match my one truth file exactly?
6. Does it promise a result I cannot control?
If ANY answer fails, the post does not publish until it is fixed.
```

Also create your one truth file if you have not: a single doc with your current price, your offer description, and your refund promise. Everything else copies from it.

## Step 7. Credit your sources by name

**What to do:** When you use someone's idea, framework, or quote, say their name. Every time.

**How Jay did it:** Jay's standing rules file says it plainly: credit sources by name when using their ideas or quotes. In practice:

- His brand story was built using the StoryBrand framework from Donald Miller's book *Building a StoryBrand* (2017). Jay names the book and the author.
- His content direction came from his coach James Guldan. Credited by name.
- His money-story teaching material came from Tom Farrar. Credited by name.
- The reliability idea he uses came from Nic Peterson. Credited by name.

Crediting costs you nothing. It makes friends instead of enemies, and it makes you look more credible, not less. Pretending you invented everything is a fabrication like any other, and the person you copied will eventually see the post.

Two harder lessons from how Jay built his business:

First, know which part of a mentor's material you are allowed to repeat. Tom Farrar's material has two parts. Part A (the behavioral money-story ideas) is usable with credit. Part B (insurance and tax mechanics) is banned outright in Jay's content, because that territory is regulated and Jay is not licensed to teach it. Borrowed material can carry payloads you must not repeat.

Second, you cannot claim an association you cannot prove. Jay's own claims policy banned him from calling a relationship a "partnership" until there was a verifiable written agreement, and banned "author of" claims until there was published work with a link. If the receipt does not exist, the claim does not either.

**Now you:** Make a "credits" list in your rules file. Every book, mentor, video, or framework you learned from goes on it. When a post uses one of those ideas, name the source in the post. And write down any part of what you learned that you should NOT teach (anything regulated, anything the person asked you to keep private).

## Step 8. Write in the voice that keeps you honest

**What to do:** Adopt voice rules that make hype physically hard to write.

**How Jay did it:** Jay's voice rules are bans, written into the same standing rules file:

- Short sentences. Plain language.
- No hype, no flattery.
- Never em dashes (the long dash). Use periods, commas, parentheses, or restructure the sentence.
- Operator voice: write like you are explaining something to a coach, not performing for a crowd.

This is not just style. Voice and honesty are connected. Hype voice needs exaggeration to survive. Plain voice can only say what is true, which is exactly why people trust it. When every sentence is short and concrete, a fake claim has nowhere to hide.

**Now you:** Add the voice rules to your rules file. Then take your last piece of writing and run it through: cut every sentence over two lines into two sentences. Delete every word you would not say out loud to a coach. Read it out loud. If a sentence sounds like an ad, rewrite it until it sounds like you.

## Words you just learned

- **Fabricate**: to make something up and present it as fact. The one unforgivable move in this business.
- **Verify before publishing**: the flag you write in place of any number or fact you cannot prove yet. It turns honesty into a workflow step.
- **Scarcity**: limited supply. Real scarcity (a calendar, a room, an enforced cap) is fine to state. Fake scarcity is a lie with a screenshot.
- **Testimonial**: a customer's own words recommending you. Only usable word-for-word, with their written permission.
- **Consent**: written permission, collected BEFORE you publish, not after.
- **Release form**: a signed document saying exactly which story, worded exactly how, may appear exactly where.
- **Anonymized**: a story told with every identifying detail removed ("a 15-year-old in our community").
- **Pseudonymize**: change names, ages, dollar amounts, and locations in a story so nobody is identifiable. Keep only the emotional truth.
- **Claims policy**: your list of banned claim types (unproven counts, unprovable partnerships, clinical language).
- **Publish gate**: a checklist that can BLOCK a post. If the post fails a check, it does not go out until fixed.
- **Single source of truth**: the one file where your price, claims, and promises live. Everything else quotes it, so nothing goes stale.
- **Dated ruling**: a rule change written down with its date and reason. Rules never change silently.
- **Compliance line**: the plain sentence on your page that says what you are and are not ("education, not financial advice").
- **Paper trading**: practicing investing with pretend money before any real dollar moves.
- **Attribution**: naming the person whose idea you borrowed. Free, and it builds relationships instead of resentment.
- **Operator voice**: short sentences, plain words, no hype. The voice of someone doing the work, not selling the dream.
