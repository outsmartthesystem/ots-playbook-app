# Keep Score: Did It Work?

**When this chapter is done you will have:** four numbers you track every week (clicks, leads, paid, revenue), one countable link for your front door, a one-page weekly scoreboard you actually fill in, and a decision date on your calendar with the rules for that decision already written down.

---

## What you need

| Item | What it is | Cost | Time |
|---|---|---|---|
| A spreadsheet | Google Sheets or Excel. A grid of rows and columns where you type numbers. This is your whole tracking system on day one. | Free | 20 min to set up |
| Your posting accounts | Wherever you already post (from the content chapters). You need to be able to look at them and count. | Free | none extra |
| A calendar | Phone calendar is fine. You will put one weekly review and one decision date on it. | Free | 5 min |
| Optional later: Google Analytics 4 (GA4) | Google's free click-counting dashboard for a website. Only matters once you have a website. Explained in Step 4. Do not build this first. | Free | skip for now |

You do not need any paid tool to keep score. The free parts of Jay's system (the click counter and the website functions) cost nothing. His contact-list app is a paid tool he already used for other things. Yours can run on a spreadsheet and honesty.

## Why this chapter exists

Here is the single most important fact in this whole playbook.

In June 2026, Jay published 88 posts in one month. They got 55 total engagements (likes, comments, that kind of thing). Tracked link clicks: zero. New contacts: one. Revenue: zero dollars.

Eighty-eight posts. Zero dollars.

He did not know this while it was happening. He found out when he ran an honest audit in early July and pulled the real numbers. The audit's verdict was blunt: the system could not detect a click, a lead, a booking, or a dollar. Publishing was working perfectly. The business was not working at all, and nothing in the system could tell him.

That discovery forced the biggest pivot in this book. Not "post more." Not "post better." First: build a way to know. The lesson of that month: more volume is not the lever. If you cannot measure it, you cannot fix it, and worse, you can feel productive while earning nothing.

This chapter is how you avoid spending a month of your life on 88 posts that did nothing, without knowing it.

## Step 1: Write down your honest baseline

**What to do:** Before you change anything, write down your real current numbers, even if every one of them is zero.

A baseline is your starting line. If you do not know where you started, you can never prove you moved.

**How Jay did it:** The July audit pulled real numbers from his scheduling tool and his contact list instead of guessing. The honest baseline read: 88 posts, 55 engagements, 0 link clicks, 1 new contact, $0 revenue. Both his funnel (the path a stranger walks from seeing a post to paying, click to lead to paid) and his measurement got a grade of F. That stung. He wrote it down anyway, because a painful true number beats a comfortable guess every time.

The baseline paid off twice. The second payoff was a surprise. When Jay ran his first real weekly numbers pull on July 10, the payment history showed something the whole plan had missed. Earlier in the year, 20 people had paid him a total of $6,950 for an older version of his business. And nobody had bought anything since April. The scoreboard's verdict: "Revenue did not fail to start; it started, then stopped." That one honest look at old data reframed the whole question, from "prove strangers will buy" to "is reviving the 20 people who already bought a faster path?" The next week's plan became re-engaging those buyers and asking the one question only they can answer: why did you stop?

**Now you:** Open a blank spreadsheet. Write today's date. Then answer, with real numbers, not vibes:

- How many posts or messages did I put out in the last 30 days?
- How many people clicked a link I shared? (If you have no way to know, write "unknown." That is a finding, not a failure.)
- How many people gave me their contact info because of my business?
- How many people paid me?
- How many dollars came in?

Also check for old revenue before you declare zero. Did anyone ever pay you for anything related to this? Babysitting money counts if it is the business you are building. Write it down. Past buyers are data.

## Step 2: Pick the only four numbers that matter

**What to do:** From now on, everything you measure maps to one of four events. Jay calls this the 4-event model.

1. **Click.** Someone clicked your link. They showed interest with their thumb.
2. **Lead.** Someone gave you their email (or phone number, or DM'd you asking for the thing). They raised their hand.
3. **Paid.** Someone bought. Money moved.
4. **Revenue.** The actual dollars that came in, added up.

That is the whole ladder. Every rung depends on the one below it. No clicks means no leads. No leads means no sales. No sales means no revenue.

**How Jay did it:** After the June disaster, Jay defined these four events and, on July 5, 2026, built counters for all four (a click-counting link, a signup list, and automatic payment records; Steps 3 and 4 show how each works). Every measurement question in his business now maps to one rung: which posts get clicks, which front door turns clicks into leads, which page turns leads into buyers, and how many dollars each week actually produced.

The model also tells you where to look when something is broken. Jay's rule, written before any traffic went out: a miss on clicks is a hooks problem (your opening lines are not stopping anyone). Clicks without leads is a message-match problem (the post promised one thing, the page said another). Leads without sales is a page or proof problem. Diagnose at the failing rung only. Do not redesign your whole business because one rung is weak.

Jay also tracks a bonus rung between lead and paid that he calls a checkout intent: someone started the buying process but did not finish. You can add that later. Start with four.

**Now you:** Add four columns to your spreadsheet: Clicks, Leads, Paid, Revenue. Then write one sentence under each defining what counts for YOUR business. Example for a teen selling a dog-walking service: a click is someone tapping the link in your bio, a lead is a parent who messages you asking for prices, a paid is a booked and paid first walk, revenue is the dollars. Definitions matter. If you do not decide in advance what counts, you will count generously on bad weeks.

## Step 3: Give your front door one countable link

**What to do:** Your front door (the first thing you ask a stranger to do, from the offer chapters) gets exactly one link, and that link must be countable.

**How Jay did it:** Jay built a short link on his own website called a redirector. Here is the whole idea. The link /go/invest-quiz counts the click, then instantly forwards the person to the real page. The link also carries small labels on the end (called UTM tags, they look like ?utm_source=instagram) that record which app the click came from. So every click gets counted and sorted, no matter where he posted it.

Then he made rules around it, because a counter is only as good as the discipline behind it:

- **Every post carries exactly one call to action pointing at one /go link, or the post does not ship (get published).** A call to action (CTA) is the one thing you ask the reader to do. A post with no link is a post you cannot measure. This rule exists because of June: 88 posts, no way to count a single click, zero clicks recorded.
- **Spell the labels exactly the same way every time.** "instagram," never "ig." Jay learned that inconsistent spellings split your counts into piles you cannot add up. The scoreboard becomes unjoinable.
- **The same one link in every bio.** Instead of hand-building a fresh link each time (humans make typos), Jay published one link everywhere that forwards through the counter automatically.
- **Test the counter with your own click before trusting it.** One of Jay's scariest findings: a misconfigured counter drops 100 percent of clicks, and that looks exactly like nobody caring. A broken scoreboard and a failing business produce the same zero. Click your own link, then confirm the count went up by one.

**Now you:** You do not need a website or a redirector to start. You need one link per front door and a way to count it.

- If your front door is "DM me the word WALKS," your counter is you: count the DMs. Jay does a version of this himself. Two numbers on his weekly scoreboard (keyword replies and DMs sent) are hand-counted every Friday, on purpose, because that channel has no tracking code. Not everything needs a robot.
- If your front door is a link, use a link that shows you a click count. Many free link shorteners display one. Verify the tool yourself before you rely on it (click your own link, watch the number move).
- Write your one link or one keyword at the top of the spreadsheet. Every post you make points at it. One post, one ask, one link. Most of Jay's posts use a soft ask (a free quiz or download, not a buy button), and he allows at most two direct sales asks per week. But a post can never have two asks.

## Step 4: Let robots count what they can, and count the rest by hand

**What to do:** Understand the two pieces of counting plumbing Jay uses, at the "what it does" level only. Then start manual anyway.

**The click counter (GA4).** Google Analytics 4 is Google's free dashboard that stores click tallies for a website. When someone hits Jay's /go link, his own website reports the click straight to Google, which means the count works even when a visitor's phone settings try to block tracking. Think of GA4 as the notebook where every click gets a tally mark, sorted by which app it came from. One hard-won warning from Jay's build: GA4 never backfills. It only records from the moment you set it up. Clicks from before are gone forever, which is why he set it up before pushing traffic, not after.

**The sale reporter (the Stripe webhook).** Stripe is the company that processes Jay's card payments. A webhook is an automatic message one tool sends another. The instant someone pays, Stripe pings Jay's website. His website then finds that person in his contact-list app (a CRM, customer relationship manager, basically a smart address book), marks them "paid," and writes a note with the amount. So every sale records itself, with no human in the loop. One detail worth copying as a value, not as code: when this system is misconfigured, it refuses to record anything rather than guessing. It never invents a sale. Your scoreboard should work the same way. An empty cell marked "unknown" is honest. A made-up number is poison.

Jay also decided in advance which system is the source of record, meaning: when two tools disagree, which one is the truth. For money, his contact list and payment records are truth, and the analytics dashboard is a backup opinion. Decide this once so you never argue with yourself.

**Now you:** You need none of this to start. A teen with a spreadsheet can run the entire 4-event model manually:

- Clicks: read the number off your link shortener, or count DMs.
- Leads: count the people who gave you contact info this week.
- Paid: count the payments. Your payment app's history is your webhook.
- Revenue: add up the dollars.

Ten minutes a week. Build the robot version only when counting by hand starts eating real time. Jay's own system still has a manual cell: on the day he shipped his scoreboard, one column could not auto-fill yet, so he marked it "pending" and checked the dashboard by eyeball instead of faking it. The principle he followed: a working scoreboard with one pending cell beats no scoreboard.

## Step 5: Build the weekly scoreboard

**What to do:** One page, filled in once a week, same day every week. Numbers on top, three honest sentences at the bottom.

**How Jay did it:** Jay keeps one scoreboard file per week, pulled every Friday. Most cells fill from his tools; he hand-enters exactly two numbers (the keyword replies and DMs from Step 3). His columns: the week, which front door was live, posts shipped, top post, best CTA line, keyword replies, DMs sent, clicks, opt-ins (his word for leads), checkout intents, paid, revenue.

Then, and this is the part most people skip, every scoreboard ends with three narrative lines:

- **Leak of the week:** where the funnel lost the most people.
- **Learning:** the one thing the numbers taught.
- **Dial-in next week:** the single change he will make.

Not five changes. One. The scoreboard's job is to drive one decision per week.

Remember Step 1: Jay's very first Friday pull found the forgotten $6,950 and rewrote his strategy in week one. That is what a scoreboard is for. It is not homework. It is the steering wheel.

One more Jay habit worth stealing: the scoreboard comes FIRST in his week. Monday starts with reading last week's numbers, then deciding what to make. Measurement first, production second. Content made before looking at the scoreboard is content made blind.

**Now you:** Copy the template at the end of this chapter into your spreadsheet or notebook. Pick your review day and put a repeating 20-minute block on your calendar. Friday afternoon or Sunday evening both work. What matters is that it is the same time every week and you never skip it, especially on bad weeks. Bad weeks are when the scoreboard earns its keep.

## Step 6: Set targets and decision gates before you start

**What to do:** Decide, in advance and in writing, what number means "keep going," what number means "change something," and the exact date you will decide. This is called a decision gate.

Why in advance? Because after four weeks of work, your feelings will negotiate. You will want to say "it almost worked" and drift for another month. A rule you wrote before you started cannot be sweet-talked.

**How Jay did it:** Three layers, all written before traffic went out.

*Targets.* For his first two-week sprint Jay set: at least 150 tracked clicks, at least 25 leads, at least 5 checkout intents, at least 1 paid. Notice how humble that last number is. One sale. The first target's job is to prove the ladder works at all, not to get rich.

*Kill and scale rules.* Written word for word before publishing: a channel with fewer than 10 clicks after 2 full weeks gets its hooks swapped once. Still dead after week 4, it drops to maintenance (one post a week, no more effort). And the opposite direction: any post whose opening line gets at least 3 times the clicks of his typical post gets 2 more posts like it the next week. Rules for winners, not just losers.

*A dated gate.* When Jay launched a new paid offer on July 5 (a $250 session where he walks a family through their money habits), he set a decision date of August 3 and pre-wrote the outcomes. Zero sales by that date means the offer itself gets reworked. One or two sales means the offer works but what comes after it needs fixing (the follow-up, the next thing buyers are offered, called the back end). Three or more sales, plus at least one sale of the bigger back-end offer, means scale it. The date was on the calendar before the first prospect ever saw the offer. Decisions wait for data reads, not vibes.

**Now you:** In your spreadsheet, under the scoreboard, write three things:

1. **Your 2-week targets.** Small and honest. For a first-ever front door, something like: 30 clicks, 5 leads, 1 paid. Adjust to your world, but write real numbers.
2. **Your kill and scale rules.** Steal Jay's shape: "If [channel] gets fewer than X clicks in 2 weeks, I change my opening lines once. If it is still dead at week 4, it goes to one post a week. If any post gets 3 times my usual clicks, I make 2 more like it."
3. **Your gate.** Pick a date 30 days out. Put it on your calendar with an alarm. Next to it write: "If paid = 0 by this date, I change the offer, not the font." Then, and this is the discipline, when the date arrives, you actually decide. Show a parent or mentor the gate when you set it. It is much harder to quietly ignore a rule someone else has seen.

The gate also needs to know why people said no, not just that they did. Numbers tell you a rung failed. Reasons tell you what to fix. So before your gate date, open the objection log you built in the chapter "Get Your First Customers" and count the no-reasons by category. Let the loudest category pick your next move. Mostly price objections ("too expensive," "not worth it") means test the offer itself. Mostly trust objections ("who are you," "prove it works") means gather more proof. Mostly timing objections ("not right now," "maybe next month") means build a follow-up lane, a simple plan for checking back politely later. A zero paid with ten logged reasons is a map. A zero paid with no reasons is just a zero.

One warning from Jay's build: before you judge a zero, make sure the counter works. He refused to push traffic until his tracking was verified, because a click that is not logged can never be recovered, and an unlogged week looks identical to a rejected offer. Test first, then judge.

## Copy-and-adapt: your weekly scoreboard template

Copy this into a spreadsheet (one row per week) or a notebook page (one page per week). This is a simplified version of Jay's real scoreboard file. Adapt the words, keep the shape.

```
WEEKLY SCOREBOARD - Week of: ____________

FRONT DOOR THIS WEEK: ______________________ (the one link or keyword)

THE FOUR NUMBERS
  Clicks (people who clicked my link): ______
  Leads (people who gave contact info): ______
  Paid (people who bought): ______
  Revenue (dollars in): $______

EFFORT (context, not the goal)
  Posts / messages I put out: ______
  Best post this week (what was it, why did it win?): ______________________
  Hand-counted extras (DM replies, in-person asks): ______

THREE HONEST SENTENCES
  Leak of the week (where I lost the most people): _________________________
  Learning (the one thing the numbers taught me): __________________________
  Dial-in next week (the ONE change I will make): __________________________

STANDING RULES (write once, reread weekly)
  My 2-week targets: ____ clicks / ____ leads / ____ paid
  My kill rule: _____________________________________________
  My scale rule: ____________________________________________
  My decision gate date: ____________  What happens if paid = 0 by then: ____________
```

Rules for using it:

- Fill it in on your fixed day, every week, including terrible weeks.
- Never leave a cell blank because the number embarrasses you. Zero is data. "Unknown" is data. A skipped week is the only real failure.
- Never write a number you did not actually count. If you cannot verify it, write "unknown" or "pending," the way Jay's own scoreboard shipped with a pending cell.
- One dial-in per week. If you cannot pick one, your leak line is not honest enough yet.
- When a rung fails, fix that rung only. No clicks: work on opening lines. Clicks but no leads: make the post's promise match the page or profile it points to. Leads but no sales: work on the offer and the proof.

## Words you just learned

- **Baseline:** your real starting numbers, written down before you change anything, so progress is provable.
- **Funnel:** the path from stranger to buyer. Same thing as the click-leads-paid-revenue ladder in this chapter.
- **4-event model:** the only four numbers that matter: clicks, leads, paid, revenue. Every measurement maps to one.
- **Click:** someone tapped your link. Interest, proven by a thumb.
- **Lead:** someone gave you their contact info or asked for the thing. A raised hand.
- **Rung:** one step of the click-to-revenue ladder. Diagnose at the failing rung only.
- **CTA (call to action):** the one thing a post asks the reader to do. One per post, maximum.
- **Front door:** the first thing you ask a stranger to do (from the offer chapters). It gets the one countable link.
- **Redirector:** a short link you control that counts the click, then forwards the visitor to the real page.
- **UTM tags:** small labels on the end of a link (like ?utm_source=instagram) that record where the click came from. Spell them identically every time.
- **GA4 (Google Analytics 4):** Google's free dashboard that stores click tallies for a website. It never backfills: it only counts from the day you set it up.
- **Webhook:** an automatic message one tool sends another the instant something happens, like Stripe reporting a sale the second the payment clears.
- **CRM:** customer relationship manager. A smart address book that puts labels like "paid" on people so you can count them later.
- **Source of record:** the one system you decided in advance to trust when two tools disagree.
- **Scoreboard:** the one page of numbers plus three sentences (Leak, Learning, Dial-in) you fill in on the same day every week.
- **Kill/scale rules:** thresholds you write before starting that say when to stop something and when to double down, so feelings do not decide.
- **Decision gate:** a date on the calendar, set in advance, where you look at the numbers and make a pre-written call instead of drifting.
