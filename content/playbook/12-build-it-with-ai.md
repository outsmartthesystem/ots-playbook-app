# Build It with AI

When this chapter is done, you will have a working system for building your business with an AI assistant: one standing brief the AI reads every time, one truth file for your facts, and a set of habits that keep the AI fast, honest, and under your control.

## What you need

- **An AI chat tool.** Claude, ChatGPT, or similar. An AI chat tool is a website or app where you type instructions and the AI types back. Free versions exist and are enough to start. Paid plans cost money per month; check current pricing with a parent before subscribing.
- **A place to keep files.** A notes app, Google Docs, or a folder on a computer. Your briefs and truth files will live here.
- **A parent or guardian in the loop.** Accounts, payments, and anything published need an adult's eyes. That is not a training-wheels rule. Jay, an adult running a real company, uses the same gate on himself.
- **Time.** About one hour to set up your first brief and truth file. The habits in this chapter are designed to save you time every week after that.
- **Cost.** Zero dollars required to start.

One more word before the steps. An AI work period is called a **session**: you open a chat, work, and close it. Here is the most important thing nobody tells beginners: the AI forgets everything between sessions. Tomorrow's session does not remember today's decisions unless you write them down somewhere the AI will read. Almost every pattern in this chapter exists to solve that one problem.

## Step 1: Write one standing brief the AI reads every single time

**What to do.** Write one short file that tells the AI who you are, what your rules are, and what "good" looks like. Then make sure the AI reads it at the start of every session. Some tools let you save it as project instructions or custom instructions so it loads automatically. If your tool cannot do that, keep the file open and paste it in as your first message every time. Either way works. The point is that you never re-explain yourself, and the AI never drifts away from your rules.

**How Jay did it.** Jay's standing brief is a file called CLAUDE.md that his AI tool reads automatically at the start of every session. It holds his voice rules ("no hype, no flattery"), his hard safety rules, and even his default weekly task. The most important line in it is the anti-lying rule: "If a stat isn't verified, don't invent it. Say 'verify before publishing' instead." Dozens of Jay's writing and marketing sessions all started from this same brief, and each of his other projects (like his website) has its own brief that follows the same pattern. Rules that must never break live in the file, not in Jay's head.

**Now you.** Part of this file already exists. In the chapter "Rules of the Game" you wrote your standing rules file: the bans, the claims rules, the "verify before publishing" habit. That file is the heart of your brief. Do not leave it sitting as a separate second document, and do not retype it from memory. Move it in, word for word, as the Hard rules section of a new file called MY-BRIEF. One set of rules, one file. Two copies drift apart, and then you cannot tell which one the AI should obey. For the rest of the brief (who you are, your files, your voice, your default ask), use the template at the end of this chapter and fill in your own answers. From today, every AI session on your business starts with this brief.

## Step 2: Keep one truth file, and make everything quote it

**What to do.** You have met this pattern twice already. The chapter "Design Your Offer" had you build your offer truth file, the one page holding your price, promise, refund policy, and link. The chapter "Rules of the Game" wired that file into your publish gate. This step does not ask you to build anything new. It asks you to connect what you built to the AI, because the rule behind the file matters more once a forgetful assistant is doing the writing. The rule: one topic, one truth file. Every fact that shows up in many places (your price, your product name, your link, your refund promise) officially lives in exactly one file, and every post, message, flyer, and page copies from it. If a second file about the same topic ever appears, one of them is lying, and you cannot tell which by reading them. Merge or delete until one is left. When a fact changes, change the truth file first, then fix everything that quotes it. And keep the tiebreaker rule written inside the file itself: if this file and anything else disagree, this file wins.

**How Jay did it.** Jay's truth file is called offer.md. It opens by declaring itself the single source of truth. Every piece of content quotes THIS file. Never type the price or link out by hand anywhere else. If this file and any other document disagree, this file wins. When the price or promise changes, update this file first, then redo anything that quoted the old version. This rule was born from pain. Before it existed, prices lived in several documents, they drifted apart, and one document contradicted the live website. After the rule, a session transcript shows the discipline in action: when a web page and offer.md disagreed about a guarantee, the instruction was to treat the page as the bug and fix the page to match the truth file.

There is a second half to this pattern. If your rule files contradict what you are about to build, fix the rule files first, in the same work batch. Jay's website plan named a phase "Paper before paint" for exactly this reason. At the time Jay was launching a new $97 offer, but his truth file still said $97 was a wrong price left over from an old mistake. Remember the tiebreaker rule: the truth file always wins. So any obedient future session that read the stale file would have "corrected" the new $97 pages, undoing his own launch. Stale rules plus an obedient AI equals your own work getting undone. That is why you fix the rule file first, in the same sitting.

**Now you.** Do not create a second file. Open the truth file you built in "Design Your Offer." This chapter calls it MY-OFFER for short; if you named yours something else, keep your name, the file is what matters. Check that it still holds your product or service name, your price, your one-sentence promise, your refund policy, and the one link people should click. Check that the tiebreaker sentence still sits at the top: "If this file and anything else disagree, this file wins. When something changes, update this file first." Then make the one addition this step is really about: tell the AI. Add a line to your standing brief saying all prices and links come from MY-OFFER and nowhere else, and that the AI must copy them out of the file, never retype them from memory. An AI quoting from memory is exactly how a stale price escapes into a live post.

## Step 3: Ask the AI to interview you

**What to do.** When you need to figure something out about your own business (your story, your customer, your message), do not ask the AI to write it for you. Ask the AI to interview you. Tell it: "Ask me questions one at a time. Dig into my answers. Do not write anything until the interview is done." The AI is a great interviewer and a mediocre mind reader. The raw material of your business is inside you. Interviewing pulls it out; guessing makes it up.

**How Jay did it.** Twice, at the two most important moments of the build. First, when Jay built his brand story, he did not ask the AI to invent one. He ran a three-session interview with the AI using the StoryBrand framework (credit: Donald Miller, from his book Building a StoryBrand). The AI asked, Jay answered, and the answers became the brand document. Even the example customer in that document was built from three real parents Jay had actually interviewed, not from imagination. Second, when Jay's coach James Guldan needed a foundation for a six-month content plan, the work started as a 28-question interview. The answers surfaced real data Jay would never have found by asking the AI to "write a plan," including which of his past posts actually got responses and which flopped.

**Now you.** For your next big unknown (maybe "who exactly is my customer?"), open a session and type: "Interview me about [topic]. One question at a time. Follow up on my answers. When you have enough, summarize what I said back to me before writing anything." Save the finished summary as a file. It becomes raw material for everything else.

## Step 4: Before any big change, run an audit and let the findings drive the plan

**What to do.** An **audit** is an honest inspection of what you have built: what is working, what is broken, what is missing. Before you change anything important, run one. Ask the AI to inspect your business from several different angles, one angle at a time (the money path, the words, the tracking, the safety rules), and collect everything it finds into one list. Then build your plan from the findings, not from your mood. Many narrow inspections beat one general "looks good to me."

**How Jay did it.** Before the July 2026 website overhaul, Jay ran an audit with 77 separate AI sessions (he calls each one an agent), each looking at one narrow thing: one sales page at a time, the homepage, whether clicks were being counted, and whether the plan still made sense. The findings, not gut feeling, became the plan. Earlier that month, a 12-agent audit of his content system returned a brutal report card: 88 posts published in June, zero link clicks, zero dollars. The path from post to purchase was broken, and nothing was even counting whether it worked. That hurt to read. It was also the most useful document of the month, because it redirected everything. Post volume was cut in half. The effort moved to replies and direct messages. Even single judgment calls got the treatment: the question "should the homepage be scrapped or improved?" went to three separate AI sessions, each asked to argue its answer independently. All three came back with the same answer: keep it and improve it. When three independent sessions agree, you can trust the answer more.

**Now you.** You will not need 77 agents. You need the habit. Before your next big change, open a session and run four separate inspection prompts: "Audit my offer for anything unclear or unproven." "Audit my posts: which got replies, which got ignored?" "Audit my links: does every post point somewhere that works?" "Audit my rules files: do they still match what I am actually doing?" Collect the findings in one list. Rank them. That list is your plan.

## Step 5: Attack important work before you trust it

**What to do.** AI findings and AI drafts both sound confident, whether they are right or wrong. So before you act on anything important, spin up a skeptic. In a fresh session, give the AI the work and one job: prove it wrong. "Here is a plan. Attack it. Find every error, every claim without proof, every step that could fail." Some findings will die under attack. That is the point. You only act on what survives. This is called **adversarial verification**, and the attacking side is sometimes called a red team.

**How Jay did it.** In the 77-agent website audit, every single finding was then handed to an independent skeptic agent whose only job was to try to disprove it. Of 66 raw findings, 65 survived and 1 was refuted. That one killed finding matters: acting on it would have wasted a whole work session fixing a problem that did not exist. The same pattern ran on new builds, not just audits: before turning on a new sign-up form on one of his websites, Jay ran 13 separate skeptic sessions against it. They found 7 real bugs, and every one got fixed before a single visitor ever saw the form. Jay even red-teams the red team. Once, a skeptic session claimed some of his code was broken. Jay did not just accept it. He checked the skeptic's evidence first, and it turned out the skeptic had invented its "proof." Then he still checked the real code himself before moving on. Your skeptic can lie too. You are the final check.

**Now you.** Anything that touches money, a customer, or your reputation gets attacked before it ships, meaning before anyone outside your family can see it. Open a fresh session, paste the draft or plan, and prompt: "You are a skeptic. Your only job is to break this. List everything wrong, unproven, or risky. Do not be polite." Fix what survives your own judgment of the attack. Then ship.

## Step 6: Write orders documents that a future session executes

**What to do.** Split thinking from doing. In one session, work with the AI to produce a numbered instruction file: what to do, in what order, with an exact "proof of done" for every step. Then, in a later fresh session, hand over that file and say "execute this." The orders file is what lets a forgetful AI do a week of complicated work without wandering, because everything it needs is on the page.

**How Jay did it.** Jay's website overhaul plan is literally headed as implementation orders, naming who wrote the plan, who executes it, and who owns it (Jay). It opens with a section called operating rules for the executing session, marked read first. Then come the phases, as tables. Every row has the order, the exact spot to change, and an acceptance proof column: the thing you can see that proves the step worked. For a teen version, a proof might read: "the flyer is printed and taped up at the gym, and the QR code opens my page on my own phone." This is Jay's house style. His memory notes describe document after document the same way: this file "is THE orders." A proof of done matters because it replaces "I think I did it" with "here is the evidence." And the flip side is written down too: "Do not re-fix what is verified working." Re-checking closed items wastes sessions.

**Now you.** Next time a project needs more than one sitting, end the planning session with: "Turn this plan into a numbered orders document a future session can execute without any other context. For each step: what to do, exactly where, and what proof shows it is done." Save the file. Start the next session by pasting it in with the words: "Execute these orders. Report the proof for each step."

## Step 7: Save decisions to memory so nothing gets re-argued

**What to do.** At the end of every work session, spend two minutes writing the session's summary into a running file: what got built, what got decided, what links or details matter, what traps you hit, what is still open. Make the AI read this file at the start of the next session (put that instruction in your standing brief). A decision that is written down stays decided. A decision that lives only in a chat that ended will get re-argued from scratch next week.

**How Jay did it.** Jay keeps a memory index: a one-line-per-build history of the entire company. Every funnel, tool, and pivot has an entry with its status, what is still open, and traps for the next session, like a tool that quietly saves the wrong date, or, at your scale, "Instagram cut off my caption at the link, put the link first next time." A session transcript catches the habit in real time: "Let me save this to memory so it persists." His orders documents even command it: log the confirmation in memory so this stops being re-audited. The payoff is compounding speed. A new session reads the index and starts from everything ever learned, instead of from zero.

**Now you.** Create a file called MY-MEMORY. After every session, add one dated line: what you did, what you decided, what is still open, and any trap you hit. Add this line to your standing brief: "Read MY-MEMORY before we start. Do not reopen decisions recorded there unless I ask."

## Step 8: Write the do-not-touch list before the AI changes anything that works

**What to do.** Sooner or later you will ask the AI to improve something that is already built and already working: add a section to your page, freshen a post, adjust a message. Here is the danger. The AI does not know which parts of your business carry the money. Ask it to "improve the page" and it may happily rewrite the whole page, including the payment link that worked this morning. So before any change to something that works, write the do-not-touch list: a short list of the things the AI may NOT modify. The working payment link. The live page people actually visit. Emails that already went out. Put the list at the top of your instructions, and tell the AI to build the new thing alongside the old thing instead of rewriting it. Working this way is called **additive**: you add next to what works, you do not remodel what works. Then, when the AI says it is done, you check the untouched things yourself. "I did not touch it" is a claim. You verify claims.

**How Jay did it.** The first operating rule in Jay's website orders document is "additive only." Right under it, the document names the exact parts no session may edit, among them the homepage's main banner, a test that was still running, the price and guarantee on his live diagnostic offer, and the file that routes his links. The document calls these parts load-bearing. In a building, a load-bearing wall holds the structure up. Knock it out while redecorating and the house comes down. Same idea here: those parts carry the money. The pattern then shows up in his logs as proof, over and over. When his $97 side hustle offer launched, the log entry ends "diagnostic untouched." When the investing offer launched next, the entry reads "diagnostic + side-hustle untouched." When a new strategy went into one of his software tools, the note says the existing tool was untouched. He records what was NOT touched the way other people record what was built. Then he checks it, because his rules also say a change is not done until the live thing is verified working. His pages have failed to update before, even when the tool reported success. Looking is the only proof.

**Now you.** The next time you ask the AI to change something that is already public, start the session with this (copy and adapt):

```
WE ARE ADDING: [the new thing, in one sentence]

DO NOT TOUCH:
- My payment link
- My live page: [link]
- Anything already sent or published
- [anything else that currently works]

Build the new thing alongside these. Do not rewrite them.
When you finish, list exactly what you changed and confirm
everything on the do-not-touch list is unchanged.
```

After the session, run your own check: open the live page on your phone, click through to the payment screen (with your parent), reread the thing that was supposed to stay the same. Then add a line to MY-MEMORY: what changed, and what you verified still works.

## Step 9: Name your repeated jobs

**What to do.** By your third or fourth week, you will notice you are typing the same instructions to the AI every time: read my files, draft my posts, flag what needs checking. That is a repeated job, and a repeated job should not live in your memory or your typing fingers. Write the instructions down once, as one named checklist file. From then on you do not re-explain the job. You invoke it, meaning you call it by name and the name carries the whole instruction. Give the file a version number and a date at the top, like a recipe card. When you find a better way to do a step, update the file and bump the version. Now the improvement is permanent. Instructions retyped from memory drift a little every week. A named file compounds instead.

**How Jay did it.** Jay's weekly content batch (the default weekly task you saw in his standing brief back in Step 1) runs as a named command. In his AI tool the job is called /plan-week, and invoking that name is the entire instruction. The file behind the name carries a version and a date (v3, dated 2026-07-06), which tells you it has already been improved twice. His run note for the job: about ten minutes, mostly unattended. The named file also enforces his rules by itself: banned words are blocked and generic drafts are rejected before he ever reads them. And when a convention changes, the fix goes into the named file, not into that week's chat. One of his website orders literally directs a new rule to be written into the /plan-week file's own notes, because patching the recipe once beats correcting the same mistake every week forever.

**Now you.** You do not need a special tool. A named job can be a plain file the AI reads. Create a file called WEEKLY-RUN (copy and adapt):

```
# WEEKLY-RUN (v1, [today's date])
My weekly job. Do the steps in order.

1. Read MY-BRIEF, MY-OFFER, and MY-MEMORY first.
2. Interview me briefly: what happened in my business this week?
3. Draft [3] posts in my voice from my answers.
4. Mark anything unproven "verify before publishing."
5. List what is still open from MY-MEMORY.
6. STOP. I run my publish gate and approve before anything ships.
```

Each week, open a session and type: "Read WEEKLY-RUN and execute it." When a step annoys you or a better idea shows up mid-run, fix the file before you close the session and change v1 to v2 with the new date. The file is the recipe. The version tells you which recipe you ran.

## Step 10: Never accept invented facts

**What to do.** Here is the ugliest truth about AI: it invents things, confidently. Fake statistics, fake customer counts, fake guarantees, fake testimonials. This is called **fabrication** (you may also hear "hallucination"). You handle it with three layers. Ban it in your standing brief. Check every fact in anything important before it ships. And if a fabrication ever reaches something customers can see, treat it as a stop-everything emergency, not a typo.

**How Jay did it.** Layer one: his standing brief bans it flat out. No fabricated stats, quotes, names, dates, or sources. No fake scarcity, no fake urgency, no fake testimonials. Layer two: it got caught at the checking stage more than once. An AI-written strategy document arrived containing a guarantee Jay had never made; the audit caught it and struck it before it shipped. The same review process even grades Jay's own weekly content, once docking a batch for one fabricated scene and two factual errors, which were fixed before publishing. Layer three: one fabrication made it all the way to a live email. One of the emails his system sends automatically to new sign-ups told readers "We are working with a small group of founding families right now." Zero buyers had ever moved through that funnel. It was a confident, friendly, completely invented sentence. When the audit found it, deleting it became order W0-1: the first order in the entire overhaul plan, ahead of everything else.

**Now you.** The ban is not new to you. You wrote it into your rules file in "Rules of the Game," and in Step 1 you moved that file into MY-BRIEF, so the AI already reads it every session. Today, confirm it survived the move word for word (it is also in the template below). The substitute phrase comes from that chapter too: when the AI produces a number or claim you cannot prove, do not delete the draft, just mark the claim "verify before publishing" and either prove it or cut it before anything ships. What is new here is the target. Back then the writer being checked was mostly you. Now it is an AI that invents faster, more fluently, and more confidently than any person. So raise the checking, not just the rule: in anything important, every number and claim gets verified before it ships. Real numbers only, even when the real number is small. "My first three customers" beats a fake "hundreds of happy customers" in every world where you want to still be in business next year.

## Step 11: You are the editor, and you are the gate

**What to do.** You built the checklist half of this gate in "Rules of the Game": the 6-question publish gate that can block a post cold. Keep running it exactly as written. This step adds the half no checklist can do. The AI can produce unlimited drafts. That is its job. Your job is different: nothing a customer sees ships without you personally reading it and approving it. Build that approval step into your routine so it cannot be skipped, and track how good the drafts are. If you are approving most drafts with light edits, your brief is working. If you are heavily rewriting everything, do not just grind; fix the brief and add examples of what good looks like. Fix the gate, not just the post.

**How Jay did it.** The rule appears in writing everywhere in Jay's system. Operating rule for the website: Jay reads and approves every word a customer will see before it goes live. The content runbook: "nothing posts without Jay's click," with a measured target of more than 70 percent of drafts approved with light edits; if he is heavily editing more than 30 percent of drafts, he stops and improves the brief itself, adding examples of what good looks like, instead of silently fixing every batch forever. The gate extends past copy into people. Stories about real people are consent-gated: one student's story runs fully anonymized on every surface until both the teen and a parent sign a written release. And in one live session, the AI drew the line itself: it fixed the fabricated email in the files, then noted that the live version still needed Jay's approval to change. The AI drafts. The human ships.

**Now you.** Make a rule and tell your parent about it so someone can hold you to it: nothing public until I have read every word out loud. The read-aloud part is not decoration. If a sentence does not sound like you talking to a friend, rewrite it. You are not a passenger in this system. The AI is the intern. You are the editor.

## Words you just learned

- **Session**: one AI work period, from opening the chat to closing it. The AI forgets everything between sessions unless it is written in a file it reads.
- **Standing brief**: the one file the AI reads at the start of every session: who you are, your rules, what good looks like.
- **Truth file / single source of truth**: the one file where a fact officially lives. Everything else quotes it, and it wins every disagreement.
- **Audit**: an honest inspection of what you have built, done before big changes, whose findings become the plan.
- **Adversarial verification (red team)**: attacking your own work or findings with a skeptic session before trusting them. You only act on what survives.
- **Orders document**: a numbered instruction file written in one session and executed in a later one, with exact steps and proof of done for each.
- **Acceptance proof**: the observable evidence that a step actually worked, so "done" is something you can see, not something you feel.
- **Memory file**: your running log of decisions, gotchas, and open items, read at the start of every session so nothing gets re-argued.
- **Do-not-touch list**: the written list of working, load-bearing things the AI may not modify during a change. Written before the work starts, checked by you after it ends.
- **Additive (build alongside)**: the rule for changing things that work: add the new next to the old instead of rewriting the old. "Untouched" is then verified, not assumed.
- **Load-bearing**: a part your business rests on, like the payment link or the live page. Named for the wall that holds a building up. Load-bearing parts go on the do-not-touch list.
- **Named job (skill)**: a repeated job written down once as a named, versioned checklist file (like WEEKLY-RUN) that the AI executes, instead of instructions retyped from memory every week.
- **Fabrication (hallucination)**: the AI confidently inventing facts, stats, or quotes. Banned in your brief, hunted in your checks, treated as an emergency if it goes live.
- **Ship**: to make something public, where a customer can see it. Until then, it is just a draft.
- **Human gate**: the rule that nothing customer-facing ships without a human reading and approving it. In your business, that human is you.
