# Get Your First Customers

When this chapter is done you will have four things:

- a list of 20 real people who might actually care about your offer
- a short honest message sent to the first five of them
- at least one real sales conversation booked or done
- a log that captures the exact words of every person who said no

Here is the part nobody tells you. Your first customers will not come from a viral post. In Jay's real build, months of posting produced exactly $0 in sales. Behind the posts sat a whole chain of free stuff leading to a paid offer. It looked finished. But it had never actually asked anyone to pay. The thing that changed it was not more content. It was a decision, in July 2026, to make direct one-to-one outreach the primary way money comes in, with content in the supporting role. Content warms people up. A personal message closes the first sales. That is why this chapter comes before "Market with Content Every Week," not after.

| Thing | What it is | Rough cost | Time |

Total new cost: $0. Total time: two or three focused hours to build the list and send the first batch, then about 30 minutes a day replying and logging.

## First, three words you need

**Outreach is contacting one specific, named person to ask them one specific thing.** It is the opposite of posting and hoping. A post talks to everyone, which means it talks to no one in particular. Outreach has a name on it.

**A prospect is a specific person you plan to ask.** Not "teens who like sneakers." A person, with a name, that you can actually reach today.

**Warm and cold describe how well someone already knows you.** A warm prospect knows you, your family, or your work. A cold prospect is a stranger. The whole chapter turns on this difference.

## Step 1: Accept that your first customers already know you

**What to do:** Stop scanning the internet for strangers. Your first customers are almost certainly inside the circle of people who already know and trust you.

**How Jay did it:** This lesson cost him months. His original path for buyers was: free thing, then free interview, then free report, then a paid pitch at the very end. He asked someone outside the business to look at everything and say what was wrong. The answer: nobody had ever been directly asked to pay. The first moment anyone could give him money came far too late. His fix made a small direct ask the very first test, and its whole job was to answer one question: "will a qualified parent pay anything at all?" Posting cannot answer that question. Asking a named human can.

Two moves from those same few days show the principle in action.

First, the mass email he refused to send. Jay had a couple hundred email contacts and a re-introduction email written, tested, and ready to send to all of them at once. He paused it on purpose. His written reasoning: "the people worth an email are worth a personal note." Sending one mass email to collect signups was exactly the low-yield habit he was quitting, the kind where you judge success by how many people opened the email instead of how many actually talked to you. He chose personal, one-at-a-time messages instead.

Second, the list he almost forgot he had. Digging through old records, he found a real list of past buyers from earlier that year, people who together had paid him thousands of dollars, and realized nobody had contacted any of them in months. Those people, the ones who had already paid him once, instantly became his warmest and most valuable outreach list. Past customers are not "done." They are the front of the line.

**Now you:** Before the next step, just name your circles out loud or on paper: family friends, your parents' friends, coaches, teammates' parents, neighbors you have worked for, teachers who know your work, and anyone who has ever paid you for anything (babysitting, mowing, tutoring, a snack stand, anything). Strangers come later, after content has warmed them up. One safety rule that runs through this whole chapter: your parent knows every adult on your list and sees every message before your first batch goes out.

## Step 2: Write the warm list of 20

**What to do:** Write down 20 real people who know you and might care about what you built. One row per person. These get personal messages, never one mass message to the whole list.

**How Jay did it:** He built a 30-prospect list in three segments of 10: people who could introduce him to the right buyer, past contacts he already had a relationship with, and carefully chosen strangers on LinkedIn. Every prospect got one row in a tracker: name, channel (text, DM, email, call), a fit note, and a status. Two of his rules are worth copying exactly.

Rule one: **fit is scored on whether the person matches who the offer is for, not on how much you like them.** Jay tagged every row `fit:strong`, `fit:likely`, or `fit:speculative` (speculative means "this is a guess"). He messaged the strong fits first. They are the fastest path to a real yes or a real no. Your best friend is warm, but if your offer is dog walking and their family has no dog, they are not a fit. A warm non-fit gets you a kind "no thanks" that teaches you nothing.

Rule two: **track status so nobody falls through a crack.** His status ladder: queued, sent, replied, booked or paid, no-buy (with the reason), not a fit. Every person on the list is always in exactly one state.

He also aimed for a steady pace, about 10 messages sent per week, rather than one giant burst.

**Copy and adapt: your tracker**

| Name | How I know them | Channel | Fit note | Status |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| | | text / DM / email / in person | why they (or their family) might need this | queued |

**Now you:** Fill 20 rows. If you cannot get to 20, that is useful information: ask a parent or coach "who do we know who deals with [the problem my offer solves]?" and watch the list grow. Sort it so your five strongest fits are on top. Show the finished list to your parent before anything gets sent.

## Step 3: Send a short, honest message

**What to do:** Message your top five prospects, one at a time, each message personalized. Short, honest, no script-sounding hype. Every message states plainly what you built, what it costs, and gives the person an easy way out.

**How Jay did it:** His direct-ask templates share one skeleton, and it is almost aggressively un-salesy (his intro-ask messages, where he asks someone for an introduction instead of a sale, are a different shape):

1. **An honest opener.** He often names what the message is right up front, with lines like "I'll be direct, this isn't a pitch for a course."
2. **One line of real personalization.** Something specific about that person: the relationship, a post they wrote, their kid's team. Proof this is not a paste job.
3. **A plain description of the offer.** What it is, who it is for, what it costs, in normal words. Price included. Hiding the price is a trick, and tricks burn warm lists.
4. **The link.**
5. **An easy out that asks for a reason.** He ends by saying that if it is not for them, one honest line about why (timing, price, not a fit) would genuinely help him. His words: "That's more useful to me than a polite no." Every message is built to produce a yes or a real reason, and the reason is the point. It feeds the log in Step 5.

And then there is his past-buyer move, which might be the single most copyable thing in this chapter. He went back to the people who had bought from him before. He had gone silent on them for months. Instead of pretending nothing happened, he admitted it. His actual message to past members who had drifted away:

> "Hey [name], Jay here. You were part of the membership earlier this year and I never properly closed the loop when things went quiet during my rebrand. I've rebuilt everything under Outsmart the System and I'm bringing the community back, better than before. Want me to send you what's new? No pitch, I just didn't want to skip the people who were there first."

His note on why these people matter so much: "The one question these buyers can answer that nobody else can: why did you stop?" If anyone has ever bought anything from you and drifted away, that question is gold. The answer tells you what to fix.

**Copy and adapt: the warm-contact message**

> Hey [name], it's [you]. [One real line: hope the season's going well for [their kid], thanks again for [real thing], etc.]
>
> I wanted you to be one of the first to know: I started a real [thing you do]. [One plain sentence on what it is and who it helps.] It's [$X], and [your refund promise from your truth file, in one line].
>
> Here's the page if you want a look: [LINK]
>
> And if it's not for you, no worries at all. One honest line on why (timing, price, not a fit) would actually help me a lot. I'm trying to learn.

**Copy and adapt: the past-customer message** (only if someone has bought from you before)

> Hey [name], it's [you]. You were one of the first people who ever paid me for [old thing], and I never properly closed the loop when I stopped. I've rebuilt it into [new thing], better than before. Want me to send you what's new? No pitch. I just didn't want to skip the people who were there first.
>
> Also, honest question, and you're the only kind of person who can answer it: what made you stop last time? Whatever the real answer is, it helps me.

Three rules for every message you send. No hype words ("insane," "game-changing," "limited time"). No fake scarcity: if you mention limited spots, it is only because your calendar is genuinely limited. And never invent proof: no made-up customer counts, no "everyone's loving this" when three people have seen it.

**Now you:** Personalize and send five this week, one at a time, on whatever channel each relationship already lives on. A message that could be copy-pasted to anyone will be felt as copy-paste by everyone. Your parent reads the first batch before it goes out. Then update each row's status to "sent."

## Step 4: The yes-call

**What to do:** When someone replies with "tell me more" or "how does it work?", do not answer with an essay. Get on a call, or meet in person. Interest is fragile. A conversation converts it; a wall of text kills it.

**How Jay did it:** The conversation itself is the closing conversation you already learned in the "Design Your Offer" chapter: listen first, restate their problem in their words, explain plainly what they get, state the price plainly, then give them room to think. You do not need to fill the pause or talk them into anything. What this chapter adds is what happens at the moment of yes. When the person says yes, Jay's script is: "Perfect, I'll send you the enrollment link right now, takes two minutes." (His enrollment link is just his payment link. Same thing, two names.) He sends the payment link during the call, while they are still on it. His written rule: "Momentum closes; 'I'll send it later' leaks." That is how he runs it with adult buyers in his adult business. Your version, below, is gentler. He also insists the payment link exists before the first call is ever booked, so a yes never has to wait while you scramble to set something up.

**Now you:** You already have the payment link from your landing page work, so you are ready. When a "tell me more" reply arrives, offer two specific times: "Want to hop on a 15-minute call? I'm free Tuesday after 4 or Saturday morning." Run the closing conversation from "Design Your Offer." If they say yes, send the payment link while you are still on the call, so they have it in hand. It is completely fine if they finish checkout after you hang up. Just note it in your tracker and check the next day. Two teen-specific rules: a parent knows about every call you book with an adult (and joins or stays nearby for the first ones), and all money still flows through the parent-supervised payment setup you built earlier. A prospect's parent buying from your parent-supervised account is exactly how this should look.

## Step 5: Log every no in the objection log

**What to do:** Every no, every "maybe later," and every silence gets one row in a table, written down the same day. The row holds the date, who it was, their exact words, and one coded category.

First, a word to learn. An **objection** is the real reason someone did not buy: too expensive, wrong time, does not trust it yet, does not need it. An **objection log** is the table where you collect those reasons. **Verbatim** means their exact words, quoted, not your summary of their words.

**How Jay did it:** He created his objection log the same day he pivoted to outreach, because he knew the no's would start arriving and each one is data. His table has these columns:

Date | Prospect (initials) | Channel | Fit? (Y/N) | Outcome (paid / no-buy / no-reply) | Objection category | Verbatim quote | Next step

His rules for using it, adapted from his file (his product was a paid diagnostic, so where his file says "diagnostic delivered," read "sales conversation"):

- One row after every sales conversation and every outreach reply. Fill it while the words are fresh, because "paraphrase kills the signal."
- **The verbatim quote is the point.** "I'd have to run it by my wife" and "my husband thinks this is unnecessary" both code as the same category, but the exact wording tells you how to answer it next time.
- If they gave no words at all, log the behavior instead: "no reply after follow-up."
- One coded category per row. Jay's seven categories were built for his offer, which sold to parents, so his list had names like price, timing, trust, and spouse-friction (one parent wants it, the other is not sold). Yours will be different. A teen-sized starter set is under the table below. Coding is what turns a pile of anecdotes into a pattern you can act on.
- And the payoff rule: if a single objection repeats across different people, that is an offer problem to fix, not a reason to send more messages.

**Why this matters more than it looks:** the log is not a diary of rejection. It feeds the decision gate (a pre-planned checkpoint where you decide in advance what each result will make you do) that you will build in the "Keep Score" chapter. Jay set himself a 30-day checkpoint with the outcomes decided in advance. Zero paying customers would mean the offer itself needs rework. One or two would mean the offer is fine but how he describes it needs to change. Several would mean it is working, so do more of it. You cannot run that checkpoint honestly without this log, because the pattern in the no's is what tells you WHICH thing to fix. Ten price objections and zero trust objections point one direction. The reverse points another.

**Copy and adapt: your objection log**

| Date | Who (initials) | Channel | Fit? (Y/N) | Outcome | Category | Their exact words | Next step |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| | | | | paid / no-buy / no-reply | | " " | |

Teen-sized categories to start with: **price** (the cost is the blocker), **timing** (believes in it, not now), **trust** (not sure you can deliver), **fit** (not the right person for this), **no-need** (does not believe the problem is real), **ask-someone** (has to check with a spouse, partner, or parent). Add your own when a reason does not fit any bucket.

**Now you:** Make the table now, before the first no arrives, so logging is a 60-second habit instead of a project. One row per reply, same day, exact words in quotes.

## Step 6: One honest follow-up, then leave them alone

**What to do:** If someone does not reply, you get exactly one follow-up. Then you stop.

**How Jay did it:** His follow-up discipline is written into his outreach playbook as a hard rule: at most ONE follow-up per prospect, about 4 to 6 days after the first message, and it must add a reason to reply, never a guilt trip. That rules out the classics: "just bumping this" and "did you see my message?" If there is still no answer, the row gets marked no-reply and he moves on. And if scarcity is ever mentioned, it is calendar-only and true, like his own line, "I take a limited number of diagnostics each week" (a diagnostic was the offer he was selling). Never a fake countdown.

Why so strict? Because your warm list is made of real relationships, and pressure spends them. A person who was neutral about your offer becomes negative about YOU after the third nudge. One follow-up preserves the relationship, which means the door stays open for the day your offer, or their timing, changes.

**Copy and adapt: the one follow-up**

> Hey [name], one quick follow-up and then I'll leave you alone about this, promise. [One NEW thing since the last message: a finished example of your work, a happier way to say what the offer does, or a genuinely useful tip related to their situation.] The page is here if it's useful: [LINK]. And "not for me" is a totally fine answer, it actually helps me.

Notice the shape: it announces it is the last message, it adds something new instead of repeating the ask, and it makes no the easy answer.

**Now you:** Put a date in your tracker when you send each first message. Five days later, if the row still says "sent," send your one follow-up. After that, the row becomes "no-reply" and the person goes back to being someone you know, not someone you are chasing. Your content (next chapter) will keep you on their radar without you ever nudging them again.

## Words you just learned

- **Outreach:** contacting one specific, named person to ask them one specific thing, instead of posting and hoping.
- **Prospect:** a specific person you plan to ask, with a name and a way to reach them.
- **Warm / cold:** how well someone already knows you. Warm people know you or your family; cold people are strangers.
- **Warm list:** your written list of real people who already know you and might care about your offer.
- **Fit:** whether a person actually matches who your offer is for, scored separately from how much you like them.
- **Tracker:** the table with one row per prospect showing who they are and what state they are in (queued, sent, replied, paid, no-buy, no-reply).
- **Win-back:** an honest message to a past customer who drifted away, owning the silence and asking what happened.
- **Yes-call:** the short call you get on the moment someone says "tell me more," where the closing conversation happens.
- **Objection:** the real reason someone did not buy.
- **Objection log:** the table where every no is recorded with a date, a category, and the person's exact words.
- **Verbatim:** their exact words, quoted, not your summary. Paraphrase kills the signal.
- **Follow-up:** the one (and only one) polite second message you send after silence, which must add something new.
- **Decision gate:** a checkpoint with the outcomes decided in advance, where your logged results tell you whether to fix, adjust, or scale. Built fully in the "Keep Score" chapter.
