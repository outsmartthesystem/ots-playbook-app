# Build Your Brand with a StoryBrand Interview

By the end of this chapter you will have a one-page BrandScript. That is a fill-in-the-blanks document that answers five things: who your customer is, what problem you solve, why anyone should trust you, what you ask people to do, and how to describe your business in one sentence you can say out loud at a dinner table without cringing.

This chapter turns the words you already compiled into a brand. The StoryBrand Interview does not invent your customer. It analyzes the real words you collected in the chapter "Do Your Market Research (Compile the Truth)". If you have not done that chapter yet, do it first, then come back here with your Voice of the Customer sheet in hand.

## What you need

| Thing | What it is | Cost | Time |
|---|---|---|---|
| An AI chat assistant (Claude or ChatGPT) | A website or app where you type questions and an AI answers. In this chapter it plays the role of your interviewer. | Free plans are enough for this chapter | Ongoing |
| A place to write | Google Docs, Notes app, or a plain text file. One document, kept forever, updated on a schedule. | Free | Ongoing |
| 2 to 3 real conversations | Short talks with people who might actually buy from you. Not a survey. A conversation. | Free | 20 to 30 minutes each |
| The interview itself | Three sessions with the AI, run back to back or across a few days | Free | 2 to 3 hours total |
| A parent or trusted adult | To sit in on customer conversations and to read your finished BrandScript before you publish anything from it | Free | 1 hour |

One credit before we start. The framework in this chapter is StoryBrand, created by Donald Miller in his book *Building a StoryBrand* (2017). Jay did not invent it. He ran it, exactly the way you are about to, and the whole Outsmart the System brand came out the other side. Use the framework, credit the source.

## Step 1: Understand the one big idea before you touch anything

**What to do.** Learn the single rule of StoryBrand: your customer is the hero of the story. You are not. You are the guide, the experienced helper who shows up when the hero is stuck.

Most new business owners get this backwards. They make their website and posts about themselves: my product, my skills, my journey. Customers do not care about your journey. They care about their problem. StoryBrand forces you to build every message around the customer's story, with your business cast as the wise helper inside it.

A BrandScript is the fill-in-the-blanks version of that story. You answer a set of questions, one element at a time, and when you are done you have the raw material for every ad, post, website page, and sales conversation you will ever write.

**How Jay did it.** In late May 2026 Jay built the entire Outsmart the System brand foundation as a five-session sprint across four days, with AI as his interviewer and drafting partner. His files label the sessions Day 1 through Day 5 (sessions 4 and 5 landed on the same calendar day):

- Day 1 (May 25): wrote a Brand Voice Memo, the cheat sheet for how he talks (words he uses, words he bans).
- Day 2 (May 26): went on a "voice walk" (talked into his phone while walking), and turned the recording into his first six polarizing beliefs (strong opinions some people will disagree with; you will write yours in Step 13).
- Day 3 (May 27): ran the 3-session StoryBrand Interview. This is the day this chapter teaches.
- Day 4 (May 28): ran a separate Villain Finder session.
- Day 5 (May 28): a second voice walk to stress-test the beliefs and add new ones.

Notice the pattern: one session, one artifact. He never tried to build everything in one giant conversation.

**Now you.** Say this sentence out loud until it stops feeling weird: "My customer is the hero. I am the guide." Then open a new document titled `my-brandscript.md` (or a Google Doc with the same name). Everything in this chapter goes in that one file.

## Step 2: Bring the words you already gathered

**What to do.** You already did the talking. In the chapter "Do Your Market Research (Compile the Truth)" you interviewed 5 to 10 real people and sorted their exact words into a Voice of the Customer sheet. This step is where you bring that sheet and your call transcripts to the table. You are not starting from a blank page. The AI interview you are about to run reads from the words you already collected.

If you have not built that sheet yet, stop and do that chapter first. An AI can organize what you learned. It cannot learn it for you. Without the real words in front of you, the AI will happily invent a fake customer, and you will build a brand for someone who does not exist.

**How Jay did it.** Before his interview day, Jay had already talked to real parents who fit his target customer. When he sat down with the AI on May 27, the hero of his BrandScript was not invented. The script itself records it: the hero was "a synthesis of real interviewed parents." Three real parents were blended into one composite character, and the document is marked internal use only, so their details never get published. Jay's story-capture rule, quoted from his own library (the folder of brand documents he keeps, like the one you are building now): "Pseudonymize ruthlessly. Change names, dollar amounts, ages, locations. Only the emotional truth stays."

**Now you.** Open your Voice of the Customer sheet and your call transcripts and keep them beside you for the whole interview. When the AI asks about your hero, their problems, or their dream, you answer with the real sentences already sitting in your buckets, not with something you invent on the spot. Have your parent or trusted adult read the sheet with you before you feed any of it into the AI.

One rule that never bends: if you ever want to use someone's real story in public, you need their written permission first. Until then, change every identifying detail.

## Step 3: Set up the AI as your interviewer

**What to do.** Open your AI assistant and tell it to run a StoryBrand interview on you. You are not asking it to write your brand. You are asking it to ask you questions, push back on vague answers, and only write down what you actually said.

This skill (an AI interviewing you one question at a time, then summarizing what you said before anything gets written) is taught in depth in the chapter "Build It with AI"; you can read that chapter first or just follow the prompt here.

Copy and adapt this prompt:

> **COPY AND ADAPT: Interviewer setup prompt**
>
> You are my brand interviewer. We are going to build a StoryBrand BrandScript for my business, following Donald Miller's StoryBrand framework. Interview me one element at a time, in this order: hero customer, external problem, internal problem, philosophical problem, guide (empathy and authority), plan, calls to action, failure, success. Ask me one question at a time. When my answer is vague, push me for a specific scene: a time of day, an exact sentence someone said, a dollar amount. Do not invent anything I did not tell you. If I cannot answer something yet, mark it "PARKED" and move on. Here are my notes from real customer conversations: [paste your notes].

**How Jay did it.** On May 27, 2026, Jay ran the interview with Claude as three sessions in a single day. His own file header records it: "completed via 3-session StoryBrand Interview." Three habits from those sessions are worth stealing:

1. **Scenes, not adjectives.** Every element in Jay's finished BrandScript is a concrete scene with an exact quote, a time of day, or a dollar amount. Nothing is described as "frustrating" or "stressful" in the abstract.
2. **Park what you cannot answer.** Step 4 of his plan was left with a note that it was parked for refinement until he had enough real client data. He did not make something up to fill the box.
3. **Log a status header.** The finished file starts with Owner, Last updated, Status, and Source (which session produced it). Six months from now you will thank yourself.

> **COPY AND ADAPT: Status header for your BrandScript file**
>
> ```
> Owner: [your name]
> Last updated: [date]
> Status: DRAFT v0.1
> Source: StoryBrand interview sessions with [AI name] on [date]
> ```

**Now you.** Paste the interviewer prompt, attach your customer notes, and start session one. Plan on three sessions. Session one: hero, external problem, internal problem, philosophical problem. Session two: guide bio, plan, calls to action. Session three: failure scene, success scene, and your one-liner (the one-liner is Step 12; just ask for it in session three). Take breaks between them. Fresh eyes catch weak answers.

## Step 4: Element 1, the hero customer

**What to do.** Describe one specific person who represents your ideal customer. Not "teens who like sneakers." One person, with an age, a situation, and enough detail that you could make decisions by asking "would this person care?"

**How Jay did it.** Jay's hero is named Daniel. He is 46 and owns a business with several locations. The hard early years are behind him, so he can leave work at 3 PM. He has two teenage kids and more money than he will ever need. Daniel lives in a specific town, and the file even records exactly how much money he has. The key insight Jay's script records about him: he is past the survival phase, which means he finally has the time to notice what his kids are absorbing.

Two things matter about how Daniel was built. First, he is a composite: a blend of three real parents Jay had interviewed, not a fantasy. Second, Daniel is private. Jay never publishes the description anywhere. He only uses it to decide things: would Daniel care about this post? Would Daniel click this? That is what "internal use only" means, and your hero file should say it too. Daniel is a tool for Jay's thinking, not a character in his ads.

**Now you.** Answer in your interview: Who is my one person? Give them a first name, an age, a situation, and one detail that explains why they are ready to buy now. Make the first name a made-up one, not a real customer's name. (Jay kept a real first name for Daniel because his file is strictly internal and never leaves his folder. Your file might get seen by a parent, a friend, or a customer, so change the name.) If you sell to other teens, your hero might be "Maya, 15, saves birthday money but has no way to earn more, embarrassed to ask her parents for cash at the mall." Build them from your real conversations, then change the identifying details.

## Step 5: Element 2, the external problem

**What to do.** Name the visible, concrete problem your hero has. The test: could you film it? If you cannot point a camera at the problem happening, it is not external enough yet.

**How Jay did it.** Jay's external problem is a single filmable scene. Tuesday evening, ride home from soccer. Daniel's daughter holds up her phone showing $250 designer shoes. Daniel tries to turn it into a teaching moment. She sighs, rolls her eyes, says "Dad, why you always talking about money?", puts her headphones in, and goes back to scrolling. The script adds one devastating line of analysis: "She didn't fight him. She tuned him out."

That is what a finished external problem looks like. A day of the week. A dollar amount. An exact quoted sentence. You could hand it to a film crew.

**Now you.** Write your hero's problem as one scene you could film. Where are they? What day? What object is in their hand? What exact sentence gets said? If your first draft sounds like "she struggles with organization," push yourself: what does the struggle look like on a Tuesday at 4 PM?

## Step 6: Element 3, the internal problem

**What to do.** Name how the external problem makes your hero feel, and write the exact sentence they say to themselves when nobody is listening. This is the most important element in the whole script. People buy because of the internal problem. The external problem is just the excuse.

**How Jay did it.** Jay's script names the feeling ("the entrepreneur's paradox," a mix of inadequacy and fear) and then writes the sentence Daniel says to himself in the dark kitchen at 9:47 PM: "I spent my entire life grinding to give her the life I never had, and now the exact comfort I built for her is what's going to ruin her."

Notice the time stamp. 9:47 PM. Not "at night." The script even explains why this sentence matters: when a parent reads it on a page, they stop scrolling. Not because it is clever, but because it is the sentence they have been thinking and have never seen anyone else say out loud.

**Now you.** Finish this in your interview: "At [exact time], alone, my hero says to themselves: ..." Use the quotes from your real conversations. The best internal-problem sentences are ones a real person actually said to you, cleaned up and pseudonymized.

## Step 7: Element 4, the philosophical problem

**What to do.** Name the lie your brand opposes: the thing "everyone says" that is wrong, and why it is wrong. This is the biggest, deepest layer. It answers: why does this problem matter beyond one customer?

**How Jay did it.** The lie OTS opposes: "Kids are resilient. They'll figure it out." Jay's script does not just call it wrong. It explains the mechanism (the step-by-step reason WHY it is wrong, not just that it is wrong): figuring it out requires friction, and the comfort wealthy parents build has removed all the friction, so the kid never adapts. Then it names what the lie really does: it lets parents off the hook. It is not faith in the kid. It is the parent skipping the work.

That structure (the lie, the mechanism of why it is a lie, what the lie secretly does for the people repeating it) is what makes a philosophical problem sharp instead of preachy.

**Now you.** Answer: What do people around my customer say that sounds wise but is actually wrong? Why exactly is it wrong? What does believing it let people avoid doing? Keep the mechanism. A philosophical problem without a mechanism is just an opinion.

## Step 8: Element 5, the guide (that's you)

**What to do.** Write a short bio (about 150 words) that does two jobs in a strict order: empathy first, authority second. Empathy means "I know how your problem feels from the inside." Authority means "and here is the proof I can help." Most people lead with their achievements. Wrong order. Nobody cares what you have done until they believe you understand them.

**How Jay did it.** Jay's canonical bio follows a recipe you can copy beat for beat:

1. Open with empathy: "I know exactly how it feels to..." (for Jay: to look at your own teenager and have no idea how to teach them without sounding like a nagging lecture).
2. Confess your own version of the problem: "I was the kid being told 'he'll figure it out,' and I didn't, until I was 38."
3. Then, and only then, credentials: one plain sentence of proof. Jay's reads: "I spent 20 years building businesses," and then names the specific companies. Facts, no bragging words.
4. Then the bridge: "I built the diagnostic I wish someone had run on my household." The bio closes right after that with the names of the frameworks he teaches.

The whole thing runs about 150 words and reads in under a minute. It appears identically on every surface (website, LinkedIn, podcast pitches), because a bio that changes everywhere is a bio nobody remembers.

**Now you.** You are a teenager, so your authority will be smaller. That is fine. Authority is proof you have done the work, not proof you are famous. "I have mowed 30 lawns in my neighborhood" is authority. "I taught myself to edit videos and posted 50 of them" is authority. Write your bio with the same recipe: how their problem feels, your own confession, your proof, your bridge. If you do not have proof yet, that is a signal to go do the work first and write the bio after. Do not invent proof. Ever.

## Step 9: Element 6, the plan

**What to do.** Give your hero a simple numbered path from "stuck" to "fixed." Three or four steps, each with a verb. A confused customer never buys. The plan exists to make working with you feel safe and obvious.

**How Jay did it.** Jay's plan is the 4-Step Journey: Diagnose, Install Quick Wins, Build the System, Lead the Legacy. Step 1 is the concrete first purchase (book the diagnostic). Step 2 is what the customer does with what they bought. Step 3 is the deeper engagement. And Step 4 carries an honest note in the file: parked for refinement until enough real diagnostics have been delivered to know what that stage actually looks like.

Read that again. Jay shipped a brand document with a visibly unfinished step, on purpose, rather than write fiction. Parking beats faking, every time.

One more detail: the file never states a price. It says pricing lives in one separate file (his offer document) and everything else points there. Prices written in many places rot in many places. You will use the same trick when you build your offer in the offer chapter.

**Now you.** Write 3 steps maximum for your first version. Step 1 must be the exact thing a customer does first ("Text me a photo of your yard"). Step 2 is what you do. Step 3 is the result. If you catch yourself writing a step you have never actually done, mark it PARKED.

## Step 10: Element 7, calls to action

**What to do.** Write two asks. The direct call to action (CTA) is the buy button: the one thing a ready customer does ("Book a lawn cleanup"). The transitional CTA is the smaller free step for people who are interested but not ready ("Download my free spring yard checklist"). Every page and post you ever make will end with one of these two.

**How Jay did it.** Direct CTA: "Book the Family Money Diagnostic," followed by two sentences that say exactly what the customer gets (a 45-minute session and a written 1-page readout). Transitional CTA: "Download 'The 5 Money Stories,'" a free 10-minute primer (built on money-story teaching Jay credits to Tom Farrar), aimed at "the reader who wants to learn first." Two asks, each one sentence long, each starting with a verb. One note: these are the CTAs as written in the 2026 BrandScript. Jay's funnel has changed since then (his revision triggers in Step 14 are exactly why), so treat them as examples of the format, not his current offers.

**Now you.** Write both. Direct: the exact purchase, starting with a verb, plus one sentence on what they get. Transitional: something free and genuinely useful you can deliver (a checklist, a sample, a 5-minute how-to). If you cannot think of a free thing yet, park it and come back after the chapter "Get Your First Customers".

## Step 11: The failure and success scenes

**What to do.** Write two short scenes set a few years in the future. Failure: what your hero's life looks like if they never solve the problem. Success: the same moment, if they do. These are the emotional stakes of your brand, and they become the backbone of your sales page (the page or flyer where you actually ask someone to buy) and the messages you send people who said "maybe."

**How Jay did it.** Jay's failure scene is three years out: Daniel's daughter is 19, home from an expensive college, no job, a $450 shoe charge on Dad's card, and an exact quote: "Just clear the balance, please." Midway through comes the gut-punch line: "She's not in crisis. She's worse. She's fine." The success scene flips the brand's original problem scene: same daughter, same age, the same Tuesday-evening shoes moment from the external problem, except now she does the mental math herself and decides to wait, and Daniel does not have to say a word. Then the same dark kitchen at 49, with the opposite sentence.

The technique to steal: the success scene is not a different story. It is the same scene with the problem solved. Same kitchen, same time of day, opposite sentence.

**Now you.** Write both scenes, 5 to 8 sentences each. Set them 1 to 3 years out. Use the same location and moment in both. End each with the sentence your hero says (or does not have to say).

## Step 12: The one-liner and the dinner-party test

**What to do.** Compress your whole BrandScript into one sentence using this formula:

> **COPY AND ADAPT: The one-liner formula**
>
> I help [WHO, your hero] [avoid the failure] so they [get the success].

Then run the dinner-party test: say it out loud to someone who has never heard of your business. If they understand it immediately and ask a follow-up question, it passes. If you have to explain it, it fails. Rewrite and test again.

**How Jay did it.** Jay's one-liner: "I help wealthy parents make sure the life they built for their kids doesn't take away their kids' drive to build a life of their own. One they earn, decide, and lead."

His file records the quality bars: 31 words, reads in about 10 seconds, every word plain English, survives the dinner-party test. And one hidden piece of engineering: the closing verbs ("earn, decide, and lead") map directly onto steps of his 4-step plan. The sentence and the plan are the same idea at two zoom levels.

**Now you.** Draft three versions of your one-liner. Read each out loud. Cut every word a 10-year-old would not know. Test the survivor on two people at dinner tonight. Keep the one that gets a follow-up question.

## Step 13: Name your villains and file your beliefs

**What to do.** Two follow-up sessions extend your BrandScript into content fuel. Run each as its own separate AI conversation. One session, one artifact.

**Session A: the Villain Finder.** A villain is the enemy your brand fights. Not a person. A bad idea, habit, or fake solution, described like a movie character. Donald Miller calls the villain the number one device storytellers use to focus conflict. Jay ran a dedicated Villain Finder session the day after his StoryBrand interview and produced exactly three villains, one per problem layer:

- External villain: The Financial-Literacy Mirage (the app or chore chart that promises to teach kids about money without the parent doing any real work).
- Internal villain: The Lecturer's Paralysis (the inner voice that says "I'll just wait until she's older" and calls it patience).
- Philosophical villain: "They'll Figure It Out" (the boss villain, the cultural script that gives parents permission to skip the work).

Each villain is written in the same fixed template:

> **COPY AND ADAPT: Villain template**
>
> - Personified as: [what the villain looks like in the world]
> - What it says: "[a quote, in the villain's own voice]"
> - Where it wins: [one specific scene where the villain beats your customer]
> - How my business defeats it: [the mechanism, not a slogan]
> - The post hook: [the first line of a social post attacking this villain]

Jay's deployment rules, straight from his villain file: every polarizing post leads with ONE villain, never blend two villains in one piece, rotate which villain leads so you do not repeat, and save the philosophical villain (the boss) for your biggest content moments.

**Session B: polarizing beliefs.** A polarizing belief is an opinion you genuinely hold that some people will disagree with. It makes the right customers pick you and the wrong ones move along. Jay filed his first six from a voice walk, then stress-tested them in a second walk two days later. His inclusion test, five rules, quoted from his library: "1. It's testable in conversation. 2. It contradicts a mainstream position. 3. You can defend it under pushback. 4. It's specific enough to disagree with. 5. It serves the diagnostic mission." Rule 5 is written for Jay's business (his diagnostic product). For yours, swap it to: "It connects to what I actually sell." Use that version when you test your own beliefs.

His calibration line: if more than 30 percent of your audience strongly disagrees, you are alienating people, not differentiating yourself. In plain terms: if almost everyone you say it to pushes back hard, you are just making people mad, not standing for something. Your teen-scale test: say each belief to 3 people you know. If all 3 get angry (not just disagree), soften it or cut it.

One more of Jay's rules worth writing down: every belief must survive the "what if my kid reads this" test. For you, make it "what if my grandmother reads this." Beliefs punch at bad ideas, never at people.

**Now you.** Run the Villain Finder session: ask the AI to help you personify one villain for each of your three problem layers, using the template above. Then, separately, list 3 opinions you actually hold about your market that most people would push back on, and run each through the five-rule inclusion test. Cut any belief you could not defend out loud to a skeptical adult.

## Step 14: Write the usage guide and the revision triggers

**What to do.** A BrandScript that sits in a folder is decoration. Finish yours with two short sections at the bottom of the file: a usage guide (which element goes on which real surface) and revision triggers (the written conditions that tell you when to redo the interview).

**How Jay did it.** His BrandScript ends with a table mapping every element to its surfaces. Jay's surfaces are website things. Yours might be an Instagram bio, a flyer, a text message to a customer, or what you say out loud. The table shows the idea; swap in your real surfaces. The pattern, adapted:

| Element | Where it goes |
|---|---|
| One-liner | Social media bios, website headline, how you introduce yourself, email signature |
| Hero portrait | Internal use only. Never published word for word. Informs every targeting decision. |
| External problem | First paragraph of emails, opening of sales conversations, first line of posts |
| Internal problem sentence | Your website's most important subheadline (the second, smaller line of text on a page), your deepest content piece |
| Philosophical problem | Your boldest posts and essays |
| Guide bio | About page, social bios, any pitch |
| Plan | The "How It Works" section of your website |
| Direct CTA | Every page ends here |
| Transitional CTA | Top of every page, your free giveaway |
| Failure scene | Middle of your sales page, one of your follow-up emails |
| Success scene | The close of your sales page, your final follow-up email |

And his revision triggers, written into the file so the decision is automatic instead of a mood. One word first: conversion, in plain terms, means the percentage of people who see your ask and take it. Jay re-runs the StoryBrand interview when any of these happens:

- 6 months pass (the default).
- The direct CTA converts below 30 percent for 4 straight weeks.
- The hero no longer matches his actual buyers.
- A new offer (a new thing you sell) launches that the script has not accounted for.
- He changes what the business is about (a repositioning).

His villain file has its own trigger: re-evaluate a villain when it stops generating reactions.

You will learn to measure conversion in the tracking chapter. Until you can measure, the 6-month calendar trigger and the "hero no longer matches my buyers" trigger are the two you can already use.

**Now you.** Add both sections to the bottom of your BrandScript file. Copy the table and fill in your real surfaces (maybe you have no website yet; then your surfaces are your Instagram bio, your flyer, and what you say when a neighbor asks what you do). Copy the triggers and set a phone reminder for the 6-month re-run date.

## Step 15: Build your library

**What to do.** Create two small files and keep them next to your BrandScript. Together with it they start your library, the folder of brand documents you met in Step 2. The content chapter draws from this folder every single week, so stock it now while the interview is fresh in your head.

**File 1: your voice memo.** Half a page describing how you talk. Words you actually say. Words you would never say. How long your sentences run. What you sound like when you explain something to a friend. Later, when you (or an AI) draft posts, this file is the cheat sheet that keeps everything sounding like you instead of like a robot.

**File 2: your story file.** A running list of 5 to 10 true short stories from your own life: a win, a failure, a moment that changed your mind. Each story is 3 to 5 sentences long and carries the date it happened. True means true. No upgrading events to make yourself look better. And any story involving another person follows the Step 2 rule: change the identifying details.

These two files become your anchors. An anchor is a reusable building block (a story, a belief, a voice rule) that a future post, email, or page gets built from, so you never start from a blank screen. The content chapter runs on anchors. No anchors, no content.

**How Jay did it.** The Brand Voice Memo was the first document Jay wrote, on Day 1, before a single post existed. Its header says the AIs read it before producing any content, and that it gets updated quarterly. Inside: who he writes as, the exact words he uses, the exact words he bans, how his sentences flow ("Short sentences. Then longer ones. Then short again."), and a read-aloud test: if the first five sentences do not sound like something he would say to a parent over coffee, rewrite. His story library files every story into the same five-field template (the moment, the lesson, the villain it surfaces, the pattern it illustrates, the reader's "me too" moment), keeps ten brainstorm prompts on hand for blank-page days, and targets 15 or more stories, most captured on voice walks.

**Now you.** Create `my-voice-memo.md` and `my-stories.md` in the same folder as your BrandScript. Fill the voice memo from this skeleton:

> **COPY AND ADAPT: Voice memo skeleton**
>
> ```
> MY VOICE MEMO
> Owner: ______  Last updated: ______  Update every 3 months.
> Who I am when I write: ______
> Words I actually say: ______
> Words I never say (my banned list): ______
> How my sentences sound (short? long? jokes? blunt?): ______
> Read-aloud test: before I post anything, I read the first three
> sentences out loud. If it does not sound like me talking to a
> friend, I rewrite it.
> ```

Then start the story file. Use this entry format 5 to 10 times:

> **COPY AND ADAPT: Story file entry**
>
> ```
> STORY [number]: [short title]
> Date it happened: ______
> What happened (3 to 5 sentences): ______
> What it taught me (one sentence): ______
> ```

If your mind goes blank, that is normal. Jay keeps ten brainstorm prompts in his story library for exactly that moment (his are parent-flavored, like a dollar amount that became a turning point). Here are ten teen-flavored ones. These are questions to jog your memory, not claims about you. Skip any that do not fit your life:

1. A time you earned your own money, even a small amount.
2. A time you saved up for something and finally bought it (or gave up halfway).
3. A purchase you regretted within a week.
4. A time you failed at something while people were watching.
5. A time you were sure you were right and turned out to be wrong.
6. A time you taught yourself a skill nobody assigned you.
7. A time you were scared to ask (for help, for permission, for a sale) and asked anyway.
8. A time you quit something, and what quitting cost you or saved you.
9. A piece of advice from an adult that turned out right (or turned out wrong).
10. The moment you first wanted to start the thing you are building now.

Date every entry, even roughly ("summer 2024" is fine). Add a new story whenever one happens. A story file only grows.

## Words you just learned

- **StoryBrand / BrandScript**: Donald Miller's framework where your customer is the hero and your business is the guide; the BrandScript is the fill-in-the-blanks document it produces.
- **Hero (composite)**: one realistic made-up person who stands for your typical customer, blended from real people you actually talked to.
- **External / internal / philosophical problem**: what is visibly going wrong / how it makes the customer feel privately / why the situation is just plain wrong in the first place.
- **Guide**: the role your business plays in the customer's story. Empathy first, authority second.
- **CTA (call to action)**: the thing you ask people to do. Direct means buy. Transitional means take a free smaller step first.
- **Sales page**: the one page (or flyer) where you make the full case and ask someone to buy.
- **One-liner**: your business in one spoken sentence, built from the who / failure / success formula.
- **Dinner-party test**: say your one-liner to someone cold; if they get it and ask a follow-up, it passes.
- **Villain**: the bad idea or fake solution your brand fights, written like a movie character. Never a real person.
- **Polarizing belief**: a true opinion you hold that filters in the right customers and filters out the wrong ones.
- **Pseudonymize**: change names, ages, places, and dollar amounts in real stories so nobody is identifiable. Keep only the emotional truth.
- **Parked**: the honest label for a section you cannot fill in yet. The opposite of making something up.
- **Usage guide**: the table at the bottom of a brand document saying exactly which surface each element goes on.
- **Revision trigger**: a written "redo this when X happens" rule, so documents get refreshed on evidence, not vibes.
- **Conversion**: the percentage of people who see an ask and take it.
- **Voice walk**: recording yourself talking (often while walking), then mining the transcript for stories and beliefs.
- **Library**: the folder holding your BrandScript, voice memo, and story file. Every future post traces back to something in it.
- **Voice memo (brand voice)**: a half-page cheat sheet of how you talk (words you use, words you ban, how your sentences sound), so everything you publish sounds like you.
- **Story file**: your running list of true, dated stories from your own life, 3 to 5 sentences each. Raw material for future posts.
- **Anchor**: a reusable building block from your library (a story, a belief, a voice rule) that a post gets built from, so you never start from zero.
- **Brainstorm prompt**: a question you keep on hand to jog a memory loose when the story file page is blank.
