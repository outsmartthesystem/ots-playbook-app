# Make Your Offer a No-Brainer (the $100M Offer Method)

By the end of this chapter, you will take the one plain paid offer you built in "Design Your Offer" and rebuild it into a no-brainer. That means an offer so clearly worth more than its price that the right person feels a little silly saying no. You will do it without inventing a single thing. Every piece of it gets pulled from words you already collected in "Do Your Market Research (Compile the Truth)." The finished no-brainer gets written straight into your offer truth file.

This chapter's method comes from Alex Hormozi's book "$100M Offers," published in 2021. Hormozi calls the thing you are building a Grand Slam Offer. We are going to teach his method in plain teen words, and bolt an honesty gate onto every step, because the whole trick of a no-brainer is that it has to be TRUE. A fake no-brainer is just a lie with good packaging.

In the CASE method the playbook runs on (Compile, Analyze, Strategize, Execute), this chapter is the second half of Strategize. You already compiled the truth. You already have a plain offer. Now you engineer that plain offer into something irresistible, using the raw material you gathered. Then, in the next chapter, you build the website that sells it.

One reminder before you start. Keep your Voice of the Customer sheet open on the desk next to you the entire time. You are going to reach for it at almost every step. You are not brainstorming value out of thin air. You are assembling it from sentences real people already said.

## What you need

- **Your offer truth file** from "Design Your Offer." The one plain offer, the refund promise, the free front door (the free thing you offer to get the right people in the door). You are upgrading this file, not starting a new one.
- **Your Voice of the Customer sheet** from the market research chapter. This is the fuel. Its buckets are the exact inputs for the steps below: the dream bucket, the frustration bucket, the objection bucket (reasons someone might say no), and the price bucket.
- **A parent or trusted adult.** Standing rule: anything touching money, accounts, or promises to customers runs through an adult.
- **Time.** Two to three hours of honest thinking. No new tools. No spending.

## Step 1: Understand what a no-brainer offer really is

**What to do:** Learn the goal before you touch your offer. A Grand Slam Offer (we will just call it a no-brainer) is an offer so good that price stops being the argument. The buyer stops asking "is this worth the money?" because the answer is obviously yes. Your job in this chapter is to make the value so clear and so real that cost becomes a small detail instead of the whole conversation.

**How it works.** Most beginners try to win by being the cheapest. That is a trap. If the only reason to pick you is a lower price, then anyone willing to charge even less than you takes the customer. You end up lowering your price again and again until nobody is making real money. Hormozi's idea flips it. Instead of lowering the price until someone says yes, you raise the value until the price looks small next to it.

Picture two lawn-mowing flyers on the same street. The first says "Lawn mowing, $25." The second says "Your yard, handled all summer, without you ever lifting a finger. First mow within two days of your call. I bring my own mower and bags, I text a photo when it's done, and if it's not the cleanest cut on the block, you don't pay. $35 a visit." The second costs more and is far easier to say yes to. That gap, where the higher price feels like the obvious choice, is what "no-brainer" means. Nothing in the second flyer is a lie. It is the same kid with the same mower who just built and described the offer better.

**Now you:** Write one sentence at the top of your working page: "My job here is not to be the cheapest. My job is to make the value so clear and true that the price stops being the argument." You will come back to it every time you feel the urge to just cut your price.

## Step 2: Learn the Value Equation and its four levers

**What to do:** Learn the one piece of math that runs this whole chapter. It is not real arithmetic you plug numbers into. It is a way to see the four things that make any offer feel more or less valuable, so you know exactly what to push on.

**How it works.** Hormozi's Value Equation says value goes up when two things on top get bigger, and two things on the bottom get smaller. In plain words:

```
              Dream Outcome  x  Perceived Likelihood it works for me
  Value  =  ---------------------------------------------------------
                      Time Delay  x  Effort and Sacrifice
```

The x means multiply. The long line means divide. So value equals the top two multiplied together, divided by the bottom two multiplied together. You will never plug in real numbers. It is just a way to see the four things to push on.

Here are the four levers in teen language:

- **Dream Outcome (top, push UP).** The result they deeply want, described in their own words.
- **Perceived Likelihood it works for them (top, push UP).** How sure the buyer is that it will actually work for THEM, not just for someone in an ad. You raise this with honest proof.
- **Time Delay (bottom, push DOWN).** How long until they get a result. Faster feels more valuable.
- **Effort and Sacrifice (bottom, push DOWN).** How hard, confusing, or annoying it is for the buyer. Easier feels more valuable.

To make an offer a no-brainer, you push the top two up and the bottom two down. That is the entire game.

Take a weak tutoring offer and run it through the levers. Weak version: "Math tutoring, $20 an hour." Now raise the top two and lower the bottom two. Dream outcome, in the parent's own words: "my kid stops dreading math and actually walks into the test feeling ready." Perceived likelihood: "here are three students on our street I already helped, and their parents will tell you." Time delay: "we find and fix the one thing they're most stuck on in the very first session." Effort and sacrifice: "I come to you, I bring the worksheets, and I send you a two-line update after each session so you always know what's happening." Same tutor. Same subject. The offer went from forgettable to obvious, and the price barely mattered anymore.

**Now you:** Write the four levers down the side of your page: Dream Outcome, Perceived Likelihood, Time Delay, Effort and Sacrifice. Put an up arrow next to the first two and a down arrow next to the last two. The next four steps take your offer through them one at a time.

## Step 3: Raise the Dream Outcome (pull it from the dream bucket)

**What to do:** State the result your offer delivers in the customer's exact words, not yours. Do not describe what you do. Describe the future they said they wanted.

**How it works.** This is the easiest lever to fake and the easiest to do honestly, because you already did the work. Open your Voice of the Customer sheet and go to the dream bucket, the answers to the magic wand question. Those sentences are your dream outcome, already written by the people who will pay. You do not have to guess what they want. They told you.

If a parent said "I just want him to have some kind of skill that means he isn't panicking about money at twenty-five," that sentence is your dream outcome. Notice how much stronger it is than a sentence you would invent, like "learn financial literacy." Nobody lies awake wanting financial literacy. They lie awake picturing their kid not panicking. Jay's messages are full of oddly specific lines like that. That is because he pulled them from real phone calls, not from making them up at a desk.

For a lawn business, the dream outcome is not "quality lawn care." It is whatever the customer actually said, maybe "I want to pull into my driveway on a Friday and feel proud of my yard instead of guilty about it."

**Now you:** Copy the two or three strongest lines out of your dream bucket and paste them into your working page under a heading that says DREAM OUTCOME. Pick the one that gave you a small chill when you first heard it. That is the promise at the top of your offer. Do not polish it into marketing language. Their raw words already work.

## Step 4: Raise Perceived Likelihood, honestly

**What to do:** Make the buyer believe it will actually work for THEM, using real proof only. This is the lever where cheaters live, so this is where the honesty gate clamps down hardest.

**How it works.** A dream outcome the buyer does not believe is worthless. Perceived likelihood is the "will this actually work for me?" lever. Hormozi raises it with proof, and there are honest kinds of proof and banned kinds. Here is what you are allowed to use:

- **A real result.** Something that genuinely happened. If your first tutoring student went from a D to a B, and it is true, you can say it.
- **A real testimonial, with permission.** A real customer's real words, shared only after they gave you clear written permission to share them.
- **A track record.** "I have mowed lawns on this street for two summers" if you have.
- **A strong, honest guarantee.** A guarantee is itself proof, because it shows you are willing to put your own money on the line (you build this in Step 10).
- **Showing your work.** Walking the buyer through exactly how the thing works, so the process itself becomes believable.

Now the banned list, and it is absolute. No made-up results. No invented testimonials. No fake reviews. No borrowed screenshots. No "join hundreds of happy families" if you have helped four. A fabricated result is a fabricated stat, and fabricated stats are banned everywhere in this playbook. Jay once caught an outside AI trying to sneak a fake award into his writing. It got rejected right away, because his truth file only allows claims he can prove. When Jay had few results, he did not invent proof. He raised likelihood the honest way, with a strong guarantee and by showing exactly how the work is done.

If you are brand new with zero results, that is fine. You lean on the honest guarantee, on showing your work in detail, and on a first result you can create fast (that is the next step). You never lean on lies.

**Now you:** List every honest proof you actually have right now. A real result, a real permission-based quote, a real track record, or none yet. Write "none yet" if that is the truth. Then write the one line you can honestly say instead, usually your guarantee. If you list a testimonial, write next to it "have written permission: yes/no." If the answer is no, you cannot use it until it is yes.

## Step 5: Lower Time Delay (give a fast first win)

**What to do:** Build a quick early result into the offer, something the buyer gets fast, ideally in the first session or the first few days. The sooner they feel progress, the more valuable the whole thing feels.

**How it works.** Waiting kills value. An offer that pays off "in six months" feels risky and far away. An offer that hands you a small real win on day one feels safe and alive. So you find the fastest genuine win inside your offer and move it to the very front.

Jay's offers are built this way. The Teen Investing Starter does not make a family wait 30 days to feel like something happened. The teen leaves the first one-on-one session with the start of a written Starter Plan and a watchlist they can actually explain to their parent. That is a fast, concrete, day-one win. For the tutoring example, the fast win is finding and fixing the single thing the kid is most stuck on in session one, so the parent feels relief immediately instead of hoping for a better report card months later. For the lawn business, it is simple: first mow within two days of the call, not "sometime next week."

The fast win does not have to be the whole transformation. It just has to be real and come quick, so the buyer's belief and momentum both rise at once.

**Now you:** Look at your offer and find the earliest honest win you can deliver. Write one sentence: "In the first [session / 48 hours / three days], they will walk away with [the concrete quick win]." Make sure it is something you can actually deliver every single time, because now you are promising it.

---

## Step 6: Lower Effort and Sacrifice (do the hard parts for them)

**What to do:** Take the confusing, annoying, or intimidating parts of the job off the customer's plate and onto yours. The less they have to do, think about, or figure out, the more valuable your offer feels.

**How it works.** People do not just pay for results. They pay to not have to do the hard thing themselves. Every step you remove, every confusing decision you make for them, every "you don't have to worry about that, I handle it," raises the value of your offer without changing the price.

Look at how Jay named the core of his $97 offers. The most valuable piece is not the PDF or the resource kit. It is the one-on-one session with a real human who walks the teen through it. The PDFs are do-it-yourself. The live session is done-with-you, which is far easier for the buyer, so it carries most of the value. Hormozi names this spectrum: do-it-yourself (they do the work), done-with-you (you do it together), and done-for-you (you do it all). The more you shift toward done-with-you and done-for-you, the lower the buyer's effort and the higher the value.

For the lawn service, lowering effort sounds like "I bring my own mower, bags, and gas. You never have to be home. I text before I come and a photo when it's done." The customer does nothing but pay and enjoy the yard. For tutoring, it is "I come to your house, I bring every worksheet, and I tell you exactly what to review." You removed all the friction.

**Now you:** List every annoying or confusing step a customer would normally have to do to get the result. Then, next to each, write how you could do it for them or with them instead. Move as much as you realistically can onto your side. Each thing you take on is a lever you just pushed down.

## Step 7: Turn every problem into a solution

**What to do:** Pull every problem and frustration from your frustration bucket, flip each one into a solution, and decide what specific thing you would give the customer to solve it. This is the engine that generates everything you will bundle together (we call that stacking) in the next step.

**How it works.** Hormozi builds offers by listing every obstacle standing between the customer and their dream, then solving each one. You already did the listing part. Your frustration bucket is that list, in the customer's own words. So the work here is mechanical and honest: take each frustration, flip "the problem is X" into "a way to solve X," and name the concrete thing you would hand them to fix it (a checklist, a template, a lesson, a session, a task you do entirely for them).

Say a parent's frustration bucket includes "he has no idea how to even start making his own money" and "every course we tried, he quit in week two." Flip the first into "a way to help him take his first real income-earning step fast" and the thing you give is a first-week action plan plus a live kickoff session. Flip the second into "a way to keep him from quitting" and the thing you give is a weekly check-in so he is never alone with it. Every solution you just created came directly from a real sentence. You invented nothing. One caution: these flips describe what you help with, not a result you promise. Never let a flip like this turn into an earnings or income promise in your actual offer. That is the hard rule in Step 10.

Use this template. It is three columns: their problem in their words, the flip, and what you would give to solve it. Copy it and fill it from your frustration bucket.

```
PROBLEMS INTO SOLUTIONS
(pull every problem from the frustration bucket, in their exact words)

Problem (their words)              ->  Solution (flip it)            ->  What I give them
"__________________________"       ->  a way to __________________   ->  ______________________
"__________________________"       ->  a way to __________________   ->  ______________________
"__________________________"       ->  a way to __________________   ->  ______________________
"__________________________"       ->  a way to __________________   ->  ______________________
"__________________________"       ->  a way to __________________   ->  ______________________
```

Do not stop at three. Be exhaustive. Every real frustration you solve is a reason to buy. Dream big here and list more than you could ever include. You trim in the next step.

**Now you:** Fill in the template with every problem from your frustration bucket. If a frustration has no solution you can honestly deliver, leave the "what I give" blank and be honest that you cannot solve that one yet. A blank is honest. A fake solution is not.

## Step 8: Trim and stack into one bundle

**What to do:** Cut the items that cost you a lot to deliver but add little value. Keep the items that are high value to the buyer and low effort for you. Then stack the survivors into one bundle, listed with an honest value beside each, a real total, and a price set well below that total.

**How it works.** You just built a long list of solutions. Some are gold: huge value to the buyer, easy for you. Some are junk: exhausting for you, barely matter to them. Trim the junk. Keep the gold. Then present what is left as a stack, so the buyer sees everything they get in one place and the value adds up in front of their eyes. Put an honest value next to each piece. Add those values into a real total. Then set your price well below that total. Now the buyer can feel the gap between what they get and what they pay. That gap is the no-brainer feeling, and the bigger and more honest it is, the easier the yes.

Now the honesty gate, and this is the most important sentence in the chapter. Every value number in your stack must be real and defensible. A defensible value is a number you could say out loud and back up. It is either what you would really charge for that piece by itself, or the real price a similar thing actually sells for. You may never inflate a value to make the gap look bigger. You may never invent a number. A made-up value number is a fabricated stat, and fabricated stats are banned. If you cannot say WHY a value is what it is, the number is not allowed in your stack.

Here is a stack for a teen tutoring offer, built from real, defensible numbers so you can see how the "why" keeps you honest.

```
THE OFFER STACK (every value must be real and defensible)

Item                              Honest value    Why that number is defensible
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
8 one-on-one sessions             $200            I charge $25/session, 8 x $25 = $200
Custom worksheet pack             $20             comparable packs sell around $20 online
Two-line update after each        $0              real value, but I don't charge for it,
  session so the parent           (included)      so I list it honest at $0
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Real total value:                 $220
Your price:                       $160
The gap they feel:                $60
```

Notice three honest moves. The session value is defensible because it is literally what you charge times how many. The worksheet value points to a real comparable price. And the update is listed at $0 because you do not actually sell it, so inflating it would be a lie. The gap is real, which means the no-brainer feeling is real too. Copy and adapt this skeleton:

```
THE OFFER STACK (fill from your kept items only)

Item 1: ______________________    Honest value: $______   Why: ____________________
Item 2: ______________________    Honest value: $______   Why: ____________________
Item 3: ______________________    Honest value: $______   Why: ____________________
Item 4: ______________________    Honest value: $______   Why: ____________________
--------------------------------------------------------------------
Real total value:                 $______
Your price:                       $______   (set below the total)
The gap they feel:                $______
```

**Now you:** Go through your Step 7 solutions. Cross out anything that is a lot of work for you and little value to them. From what survives, fill in the stack. Write a defensible "why" next to every single value. If you cannot write an honest why, delete the number. Then set your price below the real total. You will sharpen that price in Step 13.

## Step 9: Add bonuses that kill objections

**What to do:** Add a few bonuses on top of the stack, where each bonus answers one specific objection from your objection bucket. Not random extras. Targeted ones.

**How it works.** An objection is the reason a customer might not buy, and you already collected a whole bucket of them on your research calls (the answers to "what might hold you back?"). Hormozi's move is to add a bonus that directly removes each objection, because a well-aimed bonus can raise the value more than cutting the price would. You are not guessing what worries people. You have their worries in writing. You just answer each one with a bonus.

If a parent's objection was "he'll quit after two weeks like every other program," the bonus that kills it is a weekly accountability check-in or a "we'll re-engage him for free until he's back on track" add-on. If the objection was "I don't have time to manage another thing," the bonus is a done-for-you setup so they manage nothing. If a lawn customer's objection was "what if it rains on my scheduled day," the bonus is "free automatic reschedule, no call needed." Each bonus is aimed at a real sentence someone actually said.

Remember the market research chapter had you ask one more question too: "what would make this a no-brainer for you?" The answers to that question are direct instructions for what to put here. If someone literally told you what would make it a yes, and you can honestly deliver it, build it in.

Use this template. Copy it and map each objection from your objection bucket to a bonus.

```
BONUSES THAT KILL OBJECTIONS
(pull every objection from the objection bucket, in their exact words)

Objection (their words)                 ->  Bonus that answers it (must be real)
"__________________________________"    ->  ________________________________________
"__________________________________"    ->  ________________________________________
"__________________________________"    ->  ________________________________________
```

One honesty note. A bonus is still part of your offer, so it must be real and you must deliver it. And if you put an honest value next to a bonus in your stack, that value follows the same defensible-only rule from Step 8.

**Now you:** For each objection in your bucket, write the one bonus that would erase it, and check that you can actually deliver it. Add the two or three strongest bonuses to your stack. Delete any bonus you cannot honestly provide.

## Step 10: Reverse the risk with an honest guarantee

**What to do:** Deepen the refund promise you already wrote in "Design Your Offer" into a real risk reversal, so saying yes feels safe. Then obey one hard rule about what you are never allowed to guarantee.

**How it works.** Risk reversal means moving the risk of "what if this doesn't work?" off the buyer and onto you. A strong guarantee does that, and it doubles as proof (it shows you believe in your own offer enough to put your money behind it). You already wrote a first refund promise in the last chapter. Here you make it stronger and clearer. Hormozi describes a few honest types:

- **Unconditional.** Money back, no questions asked, within a set window. The simplest and boldest. "Not happy in the first 14 days? Email me and I refund you, no questions."
- **Conditional (do-the-work).** The buyer gets the refund only if they actually did their part and still did not get the result. Fair for longer programs. This is exactly the style Jay used on his $995 programs: show up, do the first steps, and if it still was not worth it, he refunds. In his words on that page, he would rather earn it than hold your money.
- **All sales final.** For special cases where a refund does not make sense, stated plainly and up front, never hidden.

Now the hard rule, and it is not optional. Never guarantee a result you cannot control. You especially never guarantee anything about money: no "your teen will earn X," no "this doubles your investment," no income or performance promises of any kind. You cannot control whether a customer does the work. You cannot control the stock market or how things turn out. So promising those results is dishonest. And when it comes to money, this playbook never crosses that line. You guarantee what you DO control: your effort, your deliverables, your time, and your willingness to refund. That is why Jay's honest guarantees promise a written plan and a watchlist the teen can explain, not a dollar amount earned. He guarantees the work and the experience, never the fortune.

And whatever you promise, you honor it every time, instantly, without arguing. One refund handled gracefully is cheap. A reputation for fighting about refunds is expensive.

**Now you:** Take the refund promise from your truth file and upgrade it. Pick the honest type that fits (unconditional is the strong default for a first, low-price offer). Write it in one or two sentences: a clear window, a clear action the buyer takes, no tricks. Then read it once more and confirm it does not promise any result you cannot control, and zero money outcomes. Put the final wording into your truth file.

## Step 11: Add scarcity and urgency only if they are real

**What to do:** Keep this short. Hormozi uses both scarcity (a real limit on how many you can serve) and urgency (a real deadline) to make people act now. This playbook allows them only when the cap or the deadline is genuinely real and you actually enforce it.

**How it works.** Real scarcity is honest: "I can only coach three families this month" if that is truly your limit, and you stop at three. Real urgency is honest: "the cohort starts on the 15th" if it actually starts on the 15th. Fake versions are banned. No "spots left" counter that never moves. No countdown timer that resets when the page reloads. You already learned this rule in "Design Your Offer" (use honest scarcity only), and it is a hard line in the "Rules of the Game" chapter. A limit is either true or it is a lie with a font.

**Now you:** Ask one question: is anything about my offer genuinely limited? My after-school hours, my calendar, a real start date? If yes, state it in plain numbers and enforce it. If nothing is truly limited, say nothing about scarcity at all. Silence is always allowed. Fake urgency never is.

---

## Step 12: Name it

**What to do:** Give your finished offer a clear, honest name that tells the right person who it is for and what they get. No hype words.

**How it works.** A good offer name does two jobs: it says who it is for, and it says what they get. That is it. Jay's names do exactly this: "Teen Side Hustle Launch" (who: teens, what: launching a side hustle) and "Teen Investing Starter" (who: teens, what: starting to invest). You know at a glance who it is for and what happens. Nothing screams, nothing exaggerates. Compare that to a name like "Ultimate Wealth Domination Blueprint," which promises the moon and tells you nothing true. For your businesses, "Friday-Ready Lawn Care" or "Math Confidence Sessions for Middle Schoolers" beat "Premium Elite Lawn Solutions" every time. Clear beats clever, and honest beats loud.

**Now you:** Write three possible names, each of which names the person and the result. Read them to your parent and pick the clearest one. Delete any word that is hype (ultimate, elite, secret, guaranteed-rich, insane). Put the winner in your truth file as the offer's name.

---

## Step 13: Price to the value, not to the bottom

**What to do:** Set a fair, real price for the real value you deliver. Start from the price anchor a real person named in your research, keep your honest refund promise, and use no price tricks.

**How it works.** Racing to be the cheapest is a trap for two reasons. First, a price that is too low can actually make people think your offer is cheap or bad. If a tutor charges $5 an hour, parents wonder what is wrong with the tutor. A fair, confident price can make the offer feel more valuable, not less. Second, the money you charge is what lets you deliver well. Starve your price and you starve the work, and then the offer really does become low value. So you price to the value you built in your stack, not to your costs, and not to whoever is cheapest on your street.

You are not guessing at the number. Open your Voice of the Customer sheet and go to the price bucket, the real numbers real people named when you asked what they would expect to pay. That is your starting anchor. If parents said around $150 for something that solved this, then charging $160 for a stack worth $220 is an easy, honest price. The buyer can see they are getting more than they pay for. Jay tested his prices on real people instead of guessing. He offered the same thing at $250 and at $95 to see which price parents who actually wanted it would pay. You do the smaller version: anchor on what real customers told you, price below your honest total value, and keep the refund promise so the risk stays on you.

No price tricks. No fake "was $400, now $160" if it was never $400. No mystery fees added at checkout. The price is the price, it is fair, and the guarantee stands behind it.

**Now you:** Look at your price bucket and write down the real numbers people named. Set your price at or above your costs, below your honest total value, and in honest range of what real customers said they would pay. Confirm your refund promise still sits right next to it. Write the final price into your truth file, and remember the rule from "Design Your Offer": nothing states a price except the truth file.

## Put it together

Do this in one sitting, in your offer truth file. When you finish, you will have one no-brainer offer written down where every post and page can quote it. Show the finished thing to your parent and to one honest adult who will push back.

1. **Dream outcome.** Paste the strongest line from your dream bucket. Their words, not yours.
2. **Honest proof.** List the real proof you have (real result, permission-based testimonial, track record, guarantee, or "none yet, leaning on the guarantee").
3. **Fast first win.** One sentence naming the concrete win the buyer gets in the first session or first days.
4. **Effort removed.** The hard parts you now do for them or with them.
5. **The stack.** Your kept items, each with a defensible honest value and a written "why," a real total, your price below it, and the gap.
6. **Bonuses.** Two or three bonuses, each mapped to a real objection from your objection bucket.
7. **Guarantee.** Your upgraded, honest risk reversal. A window, an action, no result-you-cannot-control, no money promises.
8. **Scarcity or urgency.** Either one real, enforced limit or date, or nothing at all.
9. **Name.** The clear, hype-free name that says who it is for and what they get.
10. **Price.** The fair price, anchored on your real price bucket, below your honest total value, refund promise beside it.

Write all ten into your truth file. That is your no-brainer offer, built entirely from truth you compiled.

## Words you just learned

- **Grand Slam Offer (no-brainer offer):** an offer so clearly worth more than its price that the right person feels silly saying no. The thing you build in this chapter, from Alex Hormozi's "$100M Offers" (2021).
- **Value Equation:** Hormozi's way of seeing value: Dream Outcome times Perceived Likelihood, divided by Time Delay times Effort. Push the top two up, the bottom two down.
- **Dream Outcome:** the result the customer deeply wants, in their own words. Pulled from your dream bucket.
- **Perceived Likelihood:** how sure the buyer is that it will work for THEM. Raised only by honest proof.
- **Time Delay:** how long until the buyer gets a result. Shorter feels more valuable, so you build in a fast first win.
- **Effort and Sacrifice:** how hard or annoying the offer is for the buyer. Lower it by doing the hard parts for them or with them.
- **Fast first win:** a small, real result the buyer gets early (first session or first days) that raises belief and momentum at once.
- **Do-it-yourself / done-with-you / done-for-you:** the effort spectrum. The more you do with or for the buyer, the lower their effort and the higher the value.
- **Offer stack:** the full bundle listed in one place, each item with an honest, defensible value, a real total, and a price set below the total.
- **Defensible value:** a value number you can stand behind out loud (what you truly charge, or a real comparable price). Anything else is a fabricated stat and is banned.
- **The gap:** the difference between your honest total value and your price. The bigger and truer the gap, the easier the yes.
- **Bonus:** an extra added on top that answers one specific objection from your objection bucket. Must be real and delivered.
- **Risk reversal (guarantee):** moving the risk off the buyer and onto you. Types: unconditional, conditional (do-the-work), and all-sales-final. Never guarantees a result you cannot control, and never a money or performance outcome.
- **Price anchor:** the real number a real customer named in your research. Your starting reference for pricing, from the price bucket.
- **Price to value:** setting price against the value you deliver, not against your costs or the cheapest competitor. Too cheap can signal low value and starve the work.
