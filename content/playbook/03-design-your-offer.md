# Design Your Offer

When this chapter is done you will have four things written down: one clearly defined paid offer, a free front door that leads to it, an honest refund promise, and a one-page "offer truth file" that every post and page you ever make will quote.

## What you need

- **A place to write one file.** Google Docs, Notes, or a plain text file. Free.
- **A parent or guardian.** All the money parts of a teen business (payment accounts, refunds, taxes) run through an adult. That is our standing rule: parent always in the loop.
- **A Stripe account, later.** Stripe is a company that handles online payments for you. Opening an account is free. Stripe keeps a small cut of each sale (about 3% plus a small fixed fee per charge; check Stripe's current pricing page for the exact numbers). You do not need it to finish this chapter's worksheet. When you do need it, your parent sets it up or co-owns it.
- **Time.** Two to four hours of thinking and writing. The payment link itself takes about 5 minutes once you have something real to sell.

## Step 1: Understand the offer ladder

**What to do:** Before you design anything, learn the shape most real businesses grow into. It is called an offer ladder. An "offer" is just the deal you put in front of someone: here is what you get, here is what it costs. A ladder is a set of offers arranged cheap to expensive, so a stranger can start small and step up as trust grows.

**How Jay did it.** On May 28, 2026, before most of his business existed, Jay drafted a full six-tier ladder on paper: a free primer and mini-quiz at the bottom, a $497 interview offer, a $1,500 done-with-you program, workshops at $49 to $149, a monthly community at $29 to $49, and $5,000+ one-on-one work at the top. Here is the honest part: most of it was never built. He marked the document "NOT purchasable today" and used it as a map, not a menu.

His pricing logic for the map, in his own words: "The Floor offer is the wedge... designed for max diagnostic data and max upsell. The Done-With-You is the workhorse... where the actual transformation happens. The Community is the moat. The 1:1 is the moonshot. Free is generous."

In plain words: the cheap offer gets people in the door and teaches you about them. The mid-price program is where the real work happens. The community keeps people around. The expensive one-on-one is the long shot. The free stuff builds goodwill. (An "upsell" is offering a bigger thing to someone who already bought a small thing. A "moat" is whatever makes it hard for anyone else to pull your people away.)

And his anti-patterns, also written down that same day, before anything was built: "Don't sell Tier 2 before Tier 1... Don't bundle. Each tier is a clean atomic unit... Don't discount... Don't pre-sell what isn't built."

Translation: a tier is one rung of the ladder. Do not sell the second rung before the first one exists. Do not glue offers together into combo deals (that is bundling). Do not cut your price just to get a yes. And do not take money for something you have not built yet.

The ladder he actually built, six weeks later, looks like this:

- **Free front doors:** a free primer (a short useful PDF), 2-minute quizzes and checks, and a free parent masterclass. **Low-cost front door:** a $47 local workshop.
- **$97 entry programs:** Teen Side Hustle Launch and Teen Investing Starter. Small, real, paid.
- **$995 six-month programs:** the flagship. The entrepreneurship program and the investing mastermind. The $97 kit price credits toward these, so nobody pays twice.
- **$1,500 back-end:** the Founding Container, one-on-one work. Never sold cold. Only offered after someone has been through a program.

Notice what the built ladder shares with the paper ladder: the shape. Free taste, small paid step, big main program, premium back-end. Notice what changed: almost every price and product. The map got him moving. Reality redrew it.

**Now you:** Do not build a ladder. Sketch one. On one page, write the ladder you imagine in a year: your free thing, your first paid thing, and one bigger thing you might offer someday. Then circle exactly one rung. That is the only one you are allowed to build right now.

## Step 2: Define your one paid thing

**What to do:** Pick a single paid offer and define it completely: who it is for, what they get, what it costs, how it gets delivered, and how long it takes. One offer. Not three.

**How Jay did it.** This lesson cost Jay two months. His first funnel (a funnel is the path a stranger walks from finding you to paying you) was free at every step: free primer, then a free "Family Money Story Interview," then a free blueprint, then a free one-on-one call, and only at the very end a verbal $1,500 offer. Total revenue: $0. He paid an outsider to inspect the business and say what was broken (that is called an audit). The verdict, which he agreed with: nobody who fit the offer had ever paid anything, so demand was unproven. The first paid step came far too late. And there was no way to tell what was working.

The fix, on July 5, 2026, was to move the small paid step to the front of the path instead of the very end. He replaced the free interview with a paid Family Money Diagnostic, tested at two prices ($250 versus $95, each shown to different people, which is called an A/B test). Its job was written down plainly: answer the question "will a qualified parent pay anything at all?"

He also gated it. Before checkout, a short form checked that the buyer was actually the person the offer was built for: name and email, the teen's age, a question about what they do for work, and one sentence about what money behavior worried them. People who did not fit were politely routed to the free primer instead of the checkout page. This is called an ICP gate. ICP stands for ideal customer profile, the exact kind of customer you want.

Two days later he shipped his first $97 entry offer (Teen Side Hustle Launch), and the day after that a second one (Teen Investing Starter), each with a clear set of deliverables (a deliverable is a concrete thing the buyer receives, something you can point at): a personalized roadmap, a resource kit, and a one-on-one session. The one-on-one session with a real person was named as the core value. Not the PDFs.

One more idea shaped what he sold, and he credits it to a May 29, 2026 meeting with his mentor Brian Grasso. The move, as Jay wrote it down afterward: "Sell them what they want. Deliver what they need." Market the concrete outcome people can already name (help your kid save for a car). Deliver the deeper skill underneath once they are in. Nobody buys "principles and frameworks."

**Now you:** Fill in this sentence set for your one offer. Every blank must be specific.

> I help **[exact kind of person]** get **[concrete result they already want]** in **[time period]** for **$[price]**. They get **[deliverable 1]**, **[deliverable 2]**, and **[the live human part]**. I deliver it by **[how: in person, video call, etc.]**.

Two rules while you fill it in. First, the price is small but real. A $0 offer tests nothing; Jay's whole first funnel proved that. Second, do not promise a result you cannot control. Promise the work, the deliverables, and your time.

What you just wrote is the plain first draft of your offer. The very next chapter, "Make Your Offer a No-Brainer (the $100M Offer Method)," takes this exact offer and makes it irresistible using Alex Hormozi's method.

## Step 3: Build a free front door that leads to it

**What to do:** Design one free thing whose only job is to start a relationship with a stranger and point them at your paid offer. This is your front door: the first thing a stranger is asked to do.

**How Jay did it.** Every paid offer on his ladder has a free door in front of it. The $97 Side Hustle offer has a free Builder Gap Check (a short quiz). The $97 Investing Starter has a free Investor Profile quiz. The parent track has a free primer PDF and a free masterclass. The pattern: the free thing is quick (about 2 minutes), genuinely useful on its own, and ends by naming the paid offer as the natural next step. A total stranger who just found you is never asked to hit a buy button first.

The free thing is generous, but it is not the business. It exists to lead somewhere. When Jay's free stuff led to more free stuff, he earned $0. When it led to one clear paid offer, he had a business.

**Now you:** Design your front door on paper. It should be answerable in one sitting: a short quiz, a checklist, a free 15-minute sample of what you do, or a one-page guide. Write down (a) what it is, (b) what the person walks away with even if they never pay you, and (c) the exact sentence at the end that points to your paid offer.

## Step 4: Write an honest refund promise

**What to do:** Decide, in writing, exactly when you give the money back. A refund promise (also called a guarantee) is what lets a stranger trust a new business. It only works if it is specific and if you honor it every time without arguing.

**How Jay did it.** He wrote a different promise for each offer, matched to what the offer actually does. Three are live today. The fourth belonged to the diagnostic, which was later retired, but the promise style is still worth copying. Copy and adapt:

> **Style 1, simple time-boxed** ($97 Side Hustle): "If you feel it was not worth the $97, email jay@outsmartthesystem.org within 14 days and you will be refunded."
>
> **Style 2, outcome-based** ($97 Investing Starter): "If your teen finishes the 30 days without a written Starter Plan and a watchlist they can explain to you, full refund."
>
> **Style 3, effort-conditioned** ($995 programs): "If your teen completes the onboarding, shows up to the first two masterminds, and works the first roadmap steps, and you still feel it wasn't worth it, email me... within 30 days and I'll refund you. I'd rather earn it than hold your money."
>
> **Style 4, value-guarantee** (the diagnostic, whose deliverable was a written readout of the family's money patterns): "If the readout doesn't name a pattern you recognize, it's free."

Look at what these have in common. A number of days. A named action the buyer takes (email this address). No fine print tricks. Style 3 asks the buyer to actually do the work before claiming the refund, which is fair on a long program. Style 4 bets the whole price on the quality of one deliverable.

**Now you:** Pick the style that fits your offer and write your own version in one or two sentences. Time-boxed is the right default for a first offer. Then write the promise in every place your price appears, word for word the same, and honor it even when it stings. One angry refund handled instantly is cheap. A reputation for arguing about refunds is expensive. The next chapter, "Make Your Offer a No-Brainer," goes deeper on guarantees as "risk reversal," building on the promise you write here.

## Step 5: Create your offer truth file

**What to do:** Open a new file called something like `my-offer.md` or "My Offer, Truth File." This one file holds your current offer: the price, what is included, the refund promise, the link to buy, and the claims you are allowed to make. Everything you ever post copies from this file. Nothing else is allowed to state a price.

**How Jay did it.** His file is called `offer.md` and it sits at the top of his content folder. One word first: "copy" is the marketing word for the written words on a page, post, or ad. The rule written inside his file: every piece of content (posts, emails, bios, the copy on his sales pages) quotes THIS file. Never type a price, a "spots left" claim, or a link directly into a post or page from memory. Always copy it out of this file. If this file and any other doc conflict, this file wins. When the offer changes, update this file FIRST, then rewrite the copy that quotes it.

Why so strict? Because prices rot. When Jay retired his free interview offer, the old copy did not disappear on its own. It survived in a PDF, in his terms and privacy pages, and in an orphaned email template, and he had to sweep every surface to find it all. His standard afterward: any post, email, or page that quotes a retired price or a dead link "is a bug, not content." You fix bugs. You do not publish them.

The truth file also holds his claims rules: no made-up numbers, no fake reviews, no credentials he cannot prove. When an outside AI audit invented an award he never won, the truth file was the reason it got caught and rejected instead of published.

**Now you:** Create your truth file today, even though your offer is one sentence old. Copy and adapt this skeleton:

```
# MY OFFER, TRUTH FILE
Last updated: [date]
Rule: every post, bio, message, and page quotes THIS file.
If anything conflicts with this file, this file wins.
When the offer changes: update this file FIRST, then fix the copy.

## Current paid offer
Name:
Who it is for:
What they get (list every deliverable):
Price: $
How it is delivered:
Refund promise (exact words):
Payment link: [none yet]

## Free front door
Name:
What it is:
The sentence that points to the paid offer:

## Claims I am allowed to make
(Only things I can prove. If I cannot prove it, it is not in here.)
-

## Claims I must never make
- Made-up numbers or stats
- Fake reviews or testimonials
- Fake countdowns or fake "spots left"
-

## Front-door history
[date]: started with ...
```

That last section matters more than it looks. Keep reading.

## Step 6: Set up the payment link

**What to do:** When your offer is real and a parent is on board, create a Stripe Payment Link. Stripe is a payment company: it takes the buyer's card, handles the security, and deposits the money in the connected bank account. A Payment Link is a checkout page Stripe builds for you. No code. You just share the URL.

**How Jay did it.** Every price he has ever charged online, from the $95 diagnostic test to the $1,500 container, was built as a Stripe Payment Link. His setup checklist, copy and adapt:

> 1. In Stripe, go to the product catalog and add the product: name, description, price, one-time.
> 2. Create the Payment Link. Turn ON collecting the buyer's name and email. You can set a private limit on how many people can buy, but never show that number, or a "spots left" counter, anywhere the public can see it.
> 3. In the Payment Link settings, set the after-payment redirect. That is the page the buyer lands on right after paying. Point it at your welcome page: a simple page you make that says "you're in" and tells them exactly what happens next. Without it, the buyer lands on a bare receipt screen and wonders if anything worked.
> 4. Copy the URL.
> 5. Paste it into your truth file, so it becomes the source of truth.

One detail deserves a full explanation because it saved Jay from a real mess: the reference id. Stripe lets you attach a label to each Payment Link called a `client_reference_id`. Think of it as a name tag on the payment. Here is why it matters. When someone pays, Stripe sends an automatic message to your system saying "someone just paid." That automatic message is called a webhook (a robot messenger between two apps). Jay's webhook then labels the buyer in his contact list app. That app is called a CRM: software that keeps a list of your people and lets you put tags on them, like "paid" or "quiz-taker". The tag is what makes the right welcome email go out.

At first his webhook told products apart by the amount paid. That worked until he had TWO $97 products. Both charge exactly 9700 cents, so the amount alone could not say which one was bought. The fix: each product's Payment Link got its own reference id, and the webhook reads the name tag first, falling back to the amount only when there is no tag. If you ever have two products at the same price, the amount is not enough. Label the link.

One relief note before any of this scares you off: you do not need a webhook or a CRM to start. Stripe emails you every time someone pays, and at your size, your contact list can be a spreadsheet. This story matters later, when things get automated.

Other gotchas Jay hit so you do not have to:

- **In Stripe, once you create a price as one-time or as repeating, you cannot change it later.** You would have to make a new one. His $995 links were built as "every 6 months" subscriptions and that could not be edited afterward. He kept it and disclosed it in plain words on the page. If your offer charges again automatically, say so where the buyer can see it.
- **A paid download at a guessable web address is a free download.** His first resource kit shipped at a public URL anyone could reach. The next offer locked the kit behind a secret token only buyers receive.
- **Have the link built before the first sales conversation ever happens.** His closing script, copy and adapt: when the family says yes, "Perfect. I'll send you the enrollment link right now, takes two minutes." Send it during the call and watch for the confirmation screen before you hang up. His rule: "Momentum closes; 'I'll send it later' leaks."

**Now you:** With your parent, open the Stripe account and build the link using the five-step checklist. Give it a reference id even if you only have one product (you will not always have one product). In Stripe, a Payment Link gets its reference id by adding `?client_reference_id=your-product-name` to the end of the link URL. Have your parent search Stripe's help for "client_reference_id payment link" to confirm the current steps, since screens change. Paste the finished URL into your truth file. Then practice the yes-call script out loud once, so the moment someone real says yes, you are not fumbling.

## Step 7: Use honest scarcity only

**What to do:** Scarcity means "there is a limited amount of this." Real scarcity is fine to say. Fake scarcity is lying, and it is banned in this playbook.

**How Jay did it.** His claims rules ban fake countdown timers and "spots left" counters that never actually move. He tried numbered "founding families" caps early on, then retired them. His truth file now allows calendar-based scarcity only: real dates, like an enrollment window that actually closes or a workshop that happens on one day. A date is either true or it is not, so it cannot quietly rot into a lie the way an unmoving counter can. The bright line: state a limit only when it is real and you actually honor it. A counter that never moves is a lie with a font.

When he wanted urgency without a countdown, he wrote the plain truth instead: the price goes up as more teens finish and the proof stacks up. In his copy, he calls it "just the plain truth that this costs less now, early, than it will later," and says outright that he is not running a fake timer.

**Now you:** Look at your offer and ask: what is genuinely limited? Your hours after school are. Your calendar is. If you can only serve three customers this month, say "I can take 3 clients this month" and stop selling at three. If nothing is limited, say nothing about scarcity. Silence is always allowed. Fake urgency never is. The next chapter treats scarcity and urgency as offer enhancers and holds them to this same honest-only standard.

## Step 8: Expect pivots, and log them

**What to do:** A pivot is a deliberate change of direction based on evidence. Your first offer is a guess. Plan for the guess to be wrong in some way, and build the habit that makes changing course cheap: write down what changed and why, in the truth file, dated.

**How Jay did it.** His front door pivoted twice in five days, and both pivots are logged inside `offer.md` itself: free "Family Money Story Interview," then the paid $250 diagnostic (July 5, 2026), then teen-first programs (July 10, 2026).

- **Pivot 1 (July 5): free to paid.** Stated reasons: the outside audit's verdict was $0 on the current track, no stranger who fit the offer had ever paid anything, and the first paid step came too late. The free interview was retired and the paid diagnostic took its place.
- **Pivot 2 (July 10): parent-first to teen-first.** Stated reason, from the file: "parents want to know what is going on with their TEEN, not themselves." The $250 diagnostic was retired as the front door and demoted to a later add-on. Content now sends parents straight to the teen programs, and the teen diagnostic became a bonus included with every purchase instead of its own product.

Notice what pivoting well looks like. He did not delete the old offer and pretend it never existed; he logged the history. He shipped every new offer additively, meaning built alongside the old pages without touching them, verified by checking that nothing in the existing funnel changed. That way a new launch could never break a working one. And when the flagship $995 programs launched, the $97 kit price credited toward them, with the framing: "The kit is the front porch; this is the house. You do not pay for the porch twice."

One more habit from his July 10 pivot week: before chasing strangers, he looked backward and found 20 real past buyers from his earlier brand, worth $6,950 in past sales, whom nobody had contacted in months. His note on them: "The one question these buyers can answer that nobody else can: why did you stop?" If anyone has ever paid you for anything, even babysitting, they are your warmest list and your best research.

**Now you:** Add today's date and your starting offer to the Front-door history section of your truth file. Then set one review question and a date to ask it (Jay set a 30-day decision gate with pre-written outcomes: zero sales means rework the offer, a few sales means adjust, several means scale). When your evidence says change, change the truth file first, then everything that quotes it, and keep the old line in the history with a date. Future you will want to know what you tried.

## Step 9: The worksheet

Do this in one sitting, in your truth file. Show the finished page to your parent and to one honest adult who will push back.

1. **Your one offer.** Complete the sentence from Step 2 with no vague words. Name the deliverables. Name the live human part. Name the price.
2. **Your free front door.** What is it, what does someone get from it even if they never pay, and what exact sentence at the end points to the paid offer?
3. **Your refund promise.** Pick one of the four styles from Step 4 and write yours in one or two sentences. Number of days, the action the buyer takes, no tricks.
4. **Your truth file.** Fill in every section of the Step 5 skeleton, including the claims you may make (provable only), the claims you never make, and line one of your front-door history with today's date.
5. **Your scarcity check.** Write one sentence: either the genuinely limited thing about your offer and its real number, or "nothing is limited, so I will say nothing about scarcity."

## Words you just learned

- **Offer:** the deal you put in front of someone: what they get, what it costs.
- **Deliverable:** a concrete thing the buyer receives that you can point at: a roadmap, a kit, a session.
- **Offer ladder:** your offers arranged cheap to expensive so trust can build in steps.
- **Entry offer:** the small paid product that proves you are worth paying.
- **Flagship:** your main program, where the real transformation and real revenue live.
- **Back-end:** a premium offer sold only to people who already bought, never to strangers.
- **Front door:** the first thing a stranger is asked to do, usually free and quick.
- **Funnel:** the path from stranger to free thing to paid thing.
- **Refund promise (guarantee):** your written, specific rule for when money comes back.
- **Truth file:** the one file holding your current prices, links, claims, and promises; everything else quotes it.
- **Copy:** the marketing word for the written words on your pages, posts, emails, and ads.
- **Stripe:** a company that processes online payments for you (it keeps a small cut of each sale; check Stripe's pricing page for the current numbers).
- **Payment Link:** a checkout page Stripe builds in minutes; you share the URL, no coding.
- **After-payment redirect:** the page a buyer lands on right after paying. Set it in the Payment Link settings so it points at your welcome page, not a bare receipt.
- **Welcome page:** a simple page that tells a new buyer "you're in" and exactly what happens next.
- **Reference id (`client_reference_id`):** a name tag on a payment link so your systems know which product was bought, even when two cost the same.
- **Webhook:** an automatic message one app sends another when something happens, like "someone just paid."
- **CRM:** software that keeps your contact list and lets you tag people (like "paid") so the right follow-up happens.
- **Tag:** a label on a contact in your CRM that triggers the right next step.
- **ICP:** ideal customer profile, the exact kind of customer your offer is built for. An ICP gate checks fit before checkout.
- **A/B test:** showing two versions (like two prices) to different people and letting the results pick the winner.
- **Scarcity:** a limited supply. Only real, enforced limits may be stated. Fake counters are lying.
- **Pivot:** a deliberate, dated change of direction based on evidence, logged in your truth file.
- **Kit credit:** money spent on your small offer counts toward your big one, so nobody pays twice.
- **Additive shipping:** building new offers alongside old ones without touching them, so a launch cannot break what already works.
- **Recurring billing:** a charge that repeats automatically; if your offer does this, say so in plain words on the page.
