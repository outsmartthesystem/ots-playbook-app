# Market with Content Every Week

**What you will have when this chapter is done:** a weekly loop you can run every single week. You talk, an AI helps you turn the talking into posts, you check every piece, you schedule it, you show up to reply, and everything points at one free front door (one single free thing every link sends people to). Marketing stops being "post when I feel like it" and becomes a machine you run.

---

## What you need

| Item | What it is | Cost | Why |
|---|---|---|---|
| Phone voice recorder | The recorder app already on your phone | Free | Your raw material starts as talking, not typing |
| An AI assistant | Claude or ChatGPT. A chat tool that can read your notes and draft posts | Free tier works to start | It drafts. You approve. It never publishes on its own |
| Your brand library | The stories, beliefs, and voice memo you built in the brand chapter | Free | Every post must come from it |
| One social account you already use | Instagram, TikTok, or the platform where your people are | Free | Start where you already have humans |
| A free long-form home (optional) | Substack, a free newsletter and blog site. People subscribe with email and your essay lands in their inbox | Free | One long piece per week anchors everything |
| A scheduler (optional, later) | A tool that posts to several accounts at set times (Jay uses one called PostEverywhere) | Free to start posting by hand | Batching saves time, but hand-posting is fine for months |
| Time | One batch session plus daily replies | About 2.5 to 3 hours once a week, plus 30 to 40 minutes a day | This is the real cost. Budget it honestly |

## The loop in one picture

Every week, the same five moves:

1. **Brain dump in.** You talk into your phone about what happened this week.
2. **Seeds out.** The AI pulls out content seeds (raw post ideas) and matches each one to an anchor in your library.
3. **Drafts in your voice.** The AI writes the posts the way YOU talk, using your voice memo.
4. **Flag and check.** Anything with a number, a name, or another person's story gets flagged: verify it or get permission before it ships.
5. **Approve and schedule.** You read everything, fix the weak parts, and schedule the week. Then you show up daily to reply.

Jay's standing weekly instruction to his AI says exactly this: take my brain dump or transcript, extract content seeds in the established structure, draft posts in my voice, flag anything needing verification or consent. That one sentence is the whole job. The rest of this chapter is how to do each part well.

---

## Step 1: Start with a brain dump, not a blank page

**What to do.** Once a week, record yourself talking for 10 to 20 minutes. What happened in your business this week? What did a customer say? What went wrong? What surprised you? Do not write. Talk. Then get the recording transcribed (most AI chat tools accept audio, or use your phone's voice-to-text).

**How Jay did it.** Jay calls these "voice walks." He goes for a walk, records himself talking, and the transcript becomes raw material. His first polarizing beliefs came out of a Tuesday voice walk. His story library has ten brainstorm prompts for when the tank feels empty, like "a specific dollar amount that became a turning point." He never starts from a blank page. He starts from a transcript.

**Now you.** Set a weekly alarm. Same day, same time. Record your dump, even if it feels boring. The boring Tuesday where a customer no-showed is a post. The $12 you wasted on the wrong supplies is a post. Save every transcript in one folder.

---

## Step 2: Extract seeds and pin each one to an anchor

**What to do.** Give the AI your transcript and your library, and ask it to pull out content seeds. A seed is a raw idea that is not a post yet: a moment, an opinion, a lesson. Then require that every seed connects to exactly one anchor from your library (a story, a belief, or a framework you already wrote down). If a seed does not connect to anything, either add it to the library first or drop it.

**How Jay did it.** Jay's rule is hard: every post must trace to exactly one library anchor. His weekly generation checklist literally re-reads the entire library every run, because AI has no memory between sessions. His instruction to it: "Re-read every time. Never write from recall." Posts that come from nowhere sound like everyone else's posts. Posts that come from a real anchor sound like a person.

He also learned the well runs dry fast. After two weeks of posting, his fresh stories were exhausted. The fix was not writing thinner posts. It was a runway check (count your unused anchors before planning the week, and if you do not have enough, STOP and refill the library first) plus mining his own archive. Mining it turned 14 old pieces into 23 fresh anchors, about a month of content he already owned.

**Now you.** After each brain dump, ask the AI: "Extract every content seed from this transcript. For each one, tell me which of my stories, beliefs, or frameworks it connects to. Flag any seed that connects to nothing." Keep a simple list of unused seeds. Before you plan a week, count them. Fewer than you need? Refill first. Your old school essays, old posts, and old journal entries count as an archive too.

## Step 3: Write in the confessional voice

**What to do.** Draft every post in first person, about something real, with the mess left in. Short sentences. No emojis doing the work. No hype. This direction comes from James Guldan, the marketing coach who shaped Jay's content system: the voice that works in 2026 is stripped-down, confessional, and real. Not emoji promo posts.

**How Jay did it.** Jay had actual data on this and it was not close. His best-performing line ever: "I learned financial literacy at 38. My son is learning it at 14. That is a 24-year head start." First person. A confession. A number. Meanwhile, every third-person "your kid should..." lecture post flatlined. Zero real comments. His story rules make the same point: a story told as a flex ("I owned six businesses") backfires; the same story told as a confession lands. Always lead with the mistake, never the empire.

Guldan also pushed Jay to cover three time directions, not just one:

- **Past:** what I got wrong and what it cost me.
- **Present:** what I am building right now, including the messy middle.
- **Future:** where this is going. For Jay, future-facing posts about AI and entrepreneurship pulled hardest.

Most people only post "lessons I learned" (past). Rotating all three keeps you interesting.

**Now you.** For each post ask: is this me confessing something real, or me lecturing? If it reads like a motivational poster, rewrite it as the specific moment it came from. Then check your week has at least one past post, one present post, and one future post.

## Step 4: Win the first line (build a hooks bank)

**What to do.** The hook is the first line of a post. Its only job is to stop the scroll. Write it last, judge it hardest, and keep a running file of every hook that ever worked for you. That file is your hooks bank.

**How Jay did it.** Jay's hook gate says the first line must contain a number, an age, a named moment, or a contradiction. Real hooks that have worked for Jay:

- "Same kid. Same $450 shoes. Same kitchen. Two completely different futures."
- "My kid can solve for x. Nobody taught him to read a pay stub."
- "I learned financial literacy at 38. My son is learning it at 14. That is a 24-year head start."

Notice: dollar amounts, ages, a contradiction. No "Have you ever wondered..." openers.

The bank itself came from mining. In July Jay went back through posts from his previous brand. He kept only the hooks that had earned real reactions, and stripped out the old brand name and any claim he could not verify. That cleaned-up bank now gets read before every weekly planning session. The rule that came with it: prefer reusing proven material from the bank over inventing new hooks. What worked once is your best raw material.

**Now you.** Start a file called hooks-bank. Every time a post outperforms your normal, copy its first line in. When you draft a new post, check the bank first. Copy-and-adapt this hook test:

> **Hook gate (run on every post):** Does line one contain a number, an age, a named moment, or a contradiction? If no, rewrite line one before anything else.

## Step 5: Match the platform (and know your personal page is special)

**What to do.** The same idea gets a different shape on each surface. And your personal profile, the one your actual friends and family follow, plays a different game than a business page.

**How Jay did it.** Jay's platform rules are specific and worth copying:

- **LinkedIn (his primary platform):** 120 to 180 words, hook in the first 5 words, zero hashtags.
- **Instagram:** caption 60 to 120 words. Quote cards put the hook on the image itself, max 14 words. Carousels (swipe-through slide posts) run 6 to 8 slides, slide 1 is a hook of 10 words or fewer, one idea per slide, max 25 words per slide.
- **Short video (TikTok, Reels, Shorts):** the hook has to land in the first moments on screen.

His data also killed an assumption: on Instagram, single-sentence graphics were his best format and reels were his worst. So for a test week he ran only graphics on Instagram and kept the videos on TikTok and YouTube, where video is mandatory. Test what works on YOUR accounts instead of copying what gurus say the algorithm wants.

The personal-page discovery was the big one. Jay's business pages got all the effort and produced zero dollars. His personal Facebook profile, which he was ignoring, was the only surface with real human reactions. The fix came from the Guldan plan. Two of his seven weekly posts go on the personal profile and ask readers to comment one keyword (INVEST or HUSTLE). Then Jay personally messages every commenter within 24 hours with the free link. No automation there, ever. Two reasons: the platform's rules prohibit automating a personal profile, and the personal touch IS the point. Counting the keyword comments by hand is his tracking system. It costs nothing.

**Now you.** Pick one primary platform and learn its native shape. Then look at where real people already react to you (probably your personal account) and put a keyword post there: "Comment WORD and I'll send you [your free thing]." Reply to every commenter yourself, same day.

## Step 6: Anchor the week with one long piece

**What to do.** Once a week, publish one long piece: an essay of several hundred words on a free platform like Substack. Short posts get attention. The long piece builds trust and gives you a mine to dig from later. Everything else in the week can be carved out of it.

**How Jay did it.** Jay publishes one essay every Monday at 6:00 am his time. Each week is built ahead as a "packet," a single document containing everything, so publishing day is paste-and-click instead of thinking. Copy-and-adapt his packet format:

> **Weekly essay packet (one document per week):**
> 1. PUBLISH INSTRUCTIONS: where it goes, the email subject line (Substack emails your essay to subscribers, so the title doubles as a subject line), the exact scheduled time, and where else you will share the link.
> 2. THE ESSAY: the full long piece, ending with one soft ask (a gentle "call to action," like "reply and tell me yours") and a signature.
> 3. THREE SHORT NOTES (Mon/Wed/Fri): standalone short posts pulled from the essay, so new people can discover you.
> 4. ONE MAIN SOCIAL POST: a shorter version of the essay rewritten to fit your main platform's normal style and length, ending with a link to the full essay.

Jay pre-built 8 weeks of packets, then set a decision gate at Week 8: if the essays are bringing in subscribers or link clicks by then, continue; if the numbers are near zero, cut the essay down to 15 minutes a week of reposting old pieces. He decided the rule before seeing the results, so the read stays honest.

**Now you.** Write one essay this week about the most interesting thing in your brain dump. Then cut three short posts out of it. You just produced most of a week's content from one sitting. If you like the rhythm after a month, open a free Substack and give the essays a permanent home.

## Step 7: Make your customers the heroes (with signed permission)

**What to do.** The strongest content you will ever have is a real customer's real result. But you tell it as THEIR story, with you as the helper in the background. And you never publish a real person's story without written permission. Real people, real results, never invented. If you have no results yet, say nothing instead of making something up.

**How Jay did it.** The Guldan direction was blunt: the kids are the hero, Jay is the guide. Promote the students; that promotes Jay. His best proof story involved a 15-year-old in his program. Here is the discipline. Until BOTH the teen and a parent or guardian signed a written release (a permission form saying exactly what may be published), the story ran on every surface as "a 15-year-old in our community." No name, no photo, no identifying detail. The release form freezes the exact approved wording, so what is signed is exactly what may be published. Copy-and-adapt the permission ask Jay uses, asked at a win moment, never by mass email:

> "You built something real here. Would you let me tell this story publicly, in your words, with your signature on exactly what gets said and where it appears, and the right to pull it any time?"

And the never-fabricate rule sits over all of it. No invented testimonials, no invented numbers, no "hundreds of happy customers" you cannot count. When Jay's cleanup pass found an old stat he could not source, it did not get a pass for being probably true. It became "[STAT, verify source before publishing]" or got cut.

**Now you.** When a customer gets a result, write the story with them as the main character. Get written permission (for anyone under 18, both their signature and a parent's). Until it is signed, anonymize: "a sophomore at my school," not a name. One honest anonymous story beats ten fake-sounding claims.

## Step 8: Point the whole week at one front door

**What to do.** A front door is the single free thing all your links point to this week: a free checklist, a free 2-minute quiz, a free sample. One door per week. If you have two free things, rotate weekly instead of splitting attention.

**How Jay did it.** Jay learned this from a brutal number: in June he published 88 posts, got 55 likes and comments, and zero link clicks. Zero. Dollars: zero. The diagnosis: his links were a mess. The free offer's link went nowhere for weeks, and every post asked for something different. The rebuild set one rule: one free front door per week. Even-numbered weeks point at his free investing quiz, odd weeks at his free builder check, and the link in his bio rotates to match so post and profile never disagree. His rule for strangers who just found you (marketers call them "cold traffic"): "Cold traffic never hits a buy button first." Strangers get the free thing; the paid offer comes later, in the follow-up.

He also rations the asking. Copy-and-adapt his CTA tiers (a CTA, call to action, is the one thing a post asks the reader to do):

> **CTA rotation:** about 70% of posts ask for nothing (pure value). About 1 in 4 makes a soft ask ("save this," "comment WORD"). Maximum 2 direct asks per week ("here's the link"), never in the same slots two weeks running.

Sample CTA lines from Jay's playbook, one per job:
- Attract: "If this sounds like your house, follow along. I post what school skips."
- Engage: "Comment INVEST and I'll send you the free 2-minute Investor Profile I use with teens."
- Convert: "The free 2-minute check is here: [link]. No card. You get the result by email."
- Nurture: "Reply and tell me the one money rule your house ran when you were a kid. I read every reply."

**Now you.** Pick your one front door (from the free-offer work in the offer chapter). Every link this week goes there. Put it in your bio. Keep roughly the 70/25/5 ratio: mostly give, occasionally soft-ask, rarely direct-ask.

## Step 9: Run the gates, get approval, then schedule

**What to do.** Before anything publishes, every piece passes a fixed checklist, and a human (you, and if you are under 18, loop in a parent) reads and approves every word. AI drafts. A human ships. Then schedule the batch, but only into time windows when you can actually be present.

**How Jay did it.** Jay's weekly batch is generated in one session, then hits a Monday review gate: 25 to 35 minutes where he reads all pieces, edits the hooks (his note: hooks are the thing AI gets wrong most), and approves or rejects each one. Nothing publishes unapproved. If he finds himself heavily rewriting more than 30% of the batch two weeks running, he stops and gives the AI more examples of posts he actually liked, plus his rewrites of the bad ones, so the next batch sounds more like him.

Copy-and-adapt his quality gates, run on every single piece:

> **Quality gates:**
> 1. **Banned words:** no "journey," "unlock," "empower," "transform," "delve," "in today's fast-paced world." Make your own list of words you would never say out loud.
> 2. **Specificity:** every piece contains a dollar amount, an age, a named moment, or a time of day.
> 3. **Hook:** line one has a number, an age, a named moment, or a contradiction.
> 4. **No reruns:** no phrase of 6 or more words repeats across two pieces in the same week, and no anchor is used twice in one week. Jay logs used anchors in a simple spreadsheet (his rotation log) so followers never see the same story twice within a few weeks.
> 5. **Read-aloud test:** would you actually say this sentence to a friend? If a generic influencer could have written it, rewrite it.
> 6. **Never invent:** no made-up stats, testimonials, names, or results. Unverified number = cut it or mark "verify before publishing."

Then the scheduling lesson Jay paid for. Early on he pre-scheduled a full week of posts and went on a cruise. The posts went out on time. Result: zero sign-ups, zero calls. His runbook verdict: the posts kept going out on schedule, but nobody new signed up or reached out, because nobody was home to reply. The rule ever since: never schedule a post for a time you cannot be online. Replying to every comment in the first hour is what actually turns readers into sign-ups, and no tool can do it for you.

For scale reference, Jay's batch structure as of mid July 2026 (the current one as this chapter is written) is 11 slots per week across four platforms. His video lane (5 short vertical clips a week) feeds the reel and clip slots, and the Monday essay from Step 6 publishes on top of the grid. His June matrix ran 22 slots. The early-July audit cut that in half, because the numbers showed volume was not the lever. Replies and DMs are. His grid, day by day:

- **Monday:** LinkedIn text post plus an Instagram carousel.
- **Tuesday:** a Facebook story post.
- **Wednesday:** LinkedIn plus an Instagram reel (from his video lane).
- **Thursday:** LinkedIn proof post (a real result or receipt) plus a Facebook story.
- **Friday:** a LinkedIn "reply post" (quote one real anonymized comment from the week and answer it) plus an Instagram engagement card (a graphic whose only job is to start comments).
- **Weekend:** video clips.

One honest note: the exact grid keeps evolving. It was 22 slots in June, a different mix was planned in early July, and it settled at 11 by mid July. It will change again when the numbers say so. The shape is the part that does not change. You do not need 11 slots. You need the shape: batch once, spread across the week, show up daily.

**Now you.** Batch your week in one sitting. Run every piece through the six gates. Read everything out loud. Schedule (or hand-post) only at times you can spend the next 30 to 60 minutes replying. Reply to every comment. Every one.

## Step 10: Score the week on Friday, then refill

**What to do.** Once a week, fill in a one-page scoreboard and let it tell you what to fix. Judge the week by actions (comments, link clicks, sign-ups, sales), never by likes.

**How Jay did it.** Jay's Friday scoreboard tracks: posts shipped, top post, best CTA line, keyword replies (hand-counted), DMs sent (hand-counted), link clicks, sign-ups, and sales. Most cells fill themselves from the tracking tools Jay has set up; he hand-enters two numbers, about two minutes of work. Then he writes three sentences: the leak of the week, the learning, and next week's dial-in. He also pre-committed his kill and scale rules so Friday cannot turn into excuse-making: a channel under 10 clicks after 2 weeks gets its hooks swapped once; still dead at week 4, it drops to maintenance; a hook pulling 3 times his median gets 2 variations next week.

After scoring, two bookkeeping moves: log which anchors the week consumed (the anti-repeat ledger), and check the runway. Every 2 to 4 weeks, refill the library from new brain dumps or your archive before it runs dry.

**Now you.** Make a one-page scoreboard with whatever columns you can actually count this week, even if it is just "posts, comments, keyword replies, link clicks." Jay's numbers come from tools you do not have yet, so count what you can see with your own eyes: comments, keyword replies, and DMs. If you want to count link clicks, use a free link shortener (a tool like Bitly's free plan that gives your link a short address and shows you how many people clicked it). Fill it every Friday. Write your three sentences. Adjust one thing, not five.

## Your starter weekly plan: one story, three posts, one long piece

Do not start with 11 slots. Start with this, and run it four weeks in a row before adding anything.

**Sunday (about 90 minutes):**
1. Brain dump: record 10 to 20 minutes about your week.
2. Extract seeds with your AI. Pick ONE story from the week. That story is this week's anchor.
3. Write the long piece: one essay (400 to 800 words) telling that story in the confessional voice. Past, present, or future angle, and rotate the angle week to week.
4. Cut three short posts from the essay: one hook-forward story post, one belief or opinion post, one soft-ask post pointing at your front door (or asking for a keyword comment).
5. Run all four pieces through the six quality gates. Get your approval pass done (read aloud, fix hooks). If you are a minor, this is where a parent reads it too.

**Monday:** publish the essay. Post short piece 1. Reply to every comment for the first hour.

**Wednesday:** post short piece 2. Reply within the hour. DM anyone who commented your keyword.

**Friday:** post short piece 3. Reply. Then fill your scoreboard, write the leak, the learning, and next week's dial-in.

**All week:** every link points at your one front door. If someone's story appears, it is either signed or anonymized. Nothing invented, ever.

That is one story, three posts, one long piece, one front door. Jay runs a bigger version of exactly this loop. Size is the only difference.

## Words you just learned

- **Brain dump:** a recorded ramble about your week. The raw material every post starts from.
- **Content seed:** a raw idea pulled from a brain dump that is not a post yet.
- **Anchor:** the library item (story, belief, or framework) a post is built on. One anchor per post.
- **Confessional voice:** first person, real, mess included. The register that works, per James Guldan's direction. The opposite of lecture voice.
- **Hook:** the first line of a post. Its only job is to stop the scroll.
- **Hooks bank:** your file of first lines that already worked, reused before inventing new ones.
- **CTA (call to action):** the one thing a post asks the reader to do.
- **Front door:** the single free thing all links point at this week.
- **Keyword post:** a post asking readers to comment one word; you personally message everyone who does.
- **Batch:** producing a whole week's content in one sitting.
- **Review gate:** the human read-and-approve step before anything publishes. AI drafts, a human ships.
- **Quality gates:** the fixed checklist (banned words, specificity, hook, no reruns, read-aloud, never invent) every piece must pass.
- **Rotation log (anti-repeat ledger):** the running record of which anchors you have used, so followers never see reruns.
- **Runway check:** counting unused anchors before planning a week. Too few means refill the library, not stretch thin.
- **Packet:** one document holding a whole week's long piece, short cuts, and publish instructions, ready to paste.
- **Substack:** a free platform for publishing essays that also emails them to subscribers.
- **Scheduler:** a tool that posts to your accounts at set times. Useful later, never required on day one.
- **First-hour replies:** answering every comment within an hour of posting. The conversion engine. Never automated.
- **Consent release:** the signed written permission you need before telling a real person's story publicly. Under 18 requires both the teen's and a parent's signature.
- **Scoreboard:** the one-page Friday table of real numbers (posts, replies, clicks, sign-ups, sales) plus a leak, a learning, and a dial-in.
